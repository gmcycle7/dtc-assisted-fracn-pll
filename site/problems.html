Design Problems

Eight open-ended design problems on the DTC-assisted fractional-N PLL — these test judgment, not recall. Each one drops you into a "spec changed / part is worse than expected" situation, asks what you would do, and hides a fully worked solution that pulls the formula, the number, and a one-line verdict from the verified loop / noise / spur / cal models. Read the question, decide, then open the solution and argue with it.

How to use this page. Treat every problem as a design review. State your move before opening the solution. The solutions reuse the same anchors as the rest of the site — slide-42 budget (87.6 fs model / 87.5 fs slide), loop assumptions [A1] $f_c=1.5$ MHz, [A2] PM $=60^\circ$, and the model code in models/extras.py / frequency_domain_model.py. P1 — The spec tightens to 60 fs. Which budget line do you attack?

GIVEN

The radio team comes back: the integrated jitter spec drops from the current $87.6$ fs model to 60 fs. Budget split is VCO 51% / REF+DTC 39% / MMD 6% / SPD+GM 4% slide p42. The jitter-vs-bandwidth model has a broad optimum at $f_c^\*\approx 2.1$ MHz giving $\approx 85.6$ fs model, i.e. the present $1.5$ MHz / $87.6$ fs point is already slightly conservative.

QUESTION

Which budget line gives you the most jitter back per unit effort, and roughly what new $f_c$ (if any) helps? Is re-tuning the loop even close to enough, or do you need a circuit change?

Show worked solution

Step 1 — convert the target to a variance ratio. Jitter adds in power (variance), so the fraction of variance you must remove is

$$\frac{\sigma_{t,\text{new}}^2}{\sigma_{t,\text{old}}^2}=\left(\frac{60}{87.6}\right)^2 = 0.469,$$

i.e. you must delete 53% of the total variance. derived

Step 2 — attack the biggest line. The VCO owns 51% of the variance slide p42. Even if you drove every other source to zero you would keep the 51% VCO share, leaving $\sigma_t = 87.6\sqrt{0.51}=62.6$ fs — still above 60 fs. So the VCO line is not optional; the spec cannot be met without touching it. derived

Step 3 — how much loop re-tuning buys you. Re-centering the loop at the model optimum $f_c^\*\approx2.1$ MHz only moves $87.6\to85.6$ fs model — a 2.3% jitter improvement, because in-band the loop high-passes the VCO and low-passes REF/DTC, and we are already near the crossover sweet spot. That is nowhere near the 31% jitter (53% variance) you need. Loop tuning alone fails.

Step 4 — the cheap circuit lever. The verified VCO scaling is "multi-core $\mathcal{L}$ drops $10\log_{10}(N_{core})$ at $N_{core}\times$ power" model (Leeson) slide p50 trend. A dual core drops VCO phase noise by 3 dB → halves the VCO variance. New VCO share $=0.51\times\tfrac12=0.255$; new total variance $=0.255+0.49=0.745$ of old → $\sigma_t=87.6\sqrt{0.745}=75.6$ fs. Still short of 60. A quad core (−6 dB, ¼ variance) gives VCO share $0.128$, total $0.618$ → $\sigma_t=87.6\sqrt{0.618}=68.9$ fs. Combine quad-core VCO and halve the REF+DTC bucket (range reduction, §P3/P6): variance $=0.128+0.195+0.10=0.42$ → $\sigma_t=56.8$ fs. derived

Judgment. Attack the VCO first — it is mathematically impossible to hit 60 fs while it owns 51% of the variance; spend the power on multi-core (each ×2 cores buys 3 dB at 2× VCO power) and only then trim the REF/DTC bucket. Loop re-tuning to $f_c^\*\approx2.1$ MHz is free but worth only ~2 fs — do it, but do not expect it to carry the spec.

P2 — The VCO comes back 2× noisier. How much must $f_c$ widen, and what does it cost in REF jitter?

GIVEN

Silicon arrives and the LC-VCO measures 2× the noise power (+3 dB in $\mathcal{L}(f)$) versus the back-solved $-116.5$ dBc/Hz @ 1 MHz [A13]. Inside the loop bandwidth the VCO is high-passed and REF/DTC is low-passed, so widening $f_c$ suppresses more VCO noise but lets through more reference. Present design: $f_c=1.5$ MHz [A1], model optimum $f_c^\*\approx2.1$ MHz, REF+DTC = 39% of budget slide p42.

QUESTION

Re-optimize $f_c$ for the noisier VCO. Roughly how far does the optimum move, and what happens to the REF/DTC share when you get there?

Show worked solution

Step 1 — why $f_c$ moves at all. The in-band closed-loop jitter is the sum of a VCO term that falls with $f_c$ (more loop gain suppresses the up-converted VCO noise) and a REF/DTC term that rises with $f_c$ (wider window passes more reference + DTC noise, each multiplied by $N^2$). The optimum sits where the two slopes cancel. Scale the VCO term by 2 and the balance point shifts up. model

Step 2 — scale law for the optimum. Model the two integrated contributions near the optimum as $\sigma^2(f_c)\approx \dfrac{A_{vco}}{f_c}+B_{ref}\,f_c$ (VCO power passed below crossover $\propto 1/f_c$ for a $1/f^2$ VCO; REF power passed $\propto$ bandwidth $\propto f_c$). Minimizing gives $f_c^\*=\sqrt{A_{vco}/B_{ref}}$, so $f_c^\*\propto\sqrt{A_{vco}}$. Doubling $A_{vco}$ moves the optimum by $\sqrt2$: derived

$$f_c^\*\big|_{2\times}\;\approx\;\sqrt2\;f_c^\*\big|_{1\times}\;=\;1.414\times 2.1\ \text{MHz}\;\approx\;2.97\ \text{MHz}.$$

Step 3 — cost in REF/DTC. The REF/DTC term is $B_{ref}f_c$, so going $2.1\to2.97$ MHz multiplies the REF/DTC variance by $2.97/2.1=1.41$. At the new optimum the two terms re-balance, and the minimum jitter rises overall: at the optimum $\sigma^2_{\min}=2\sqrt{A_{vco}B_{ref}}\propto\sqrt{A_{vco}}$, so doubling the VCO power raises the achievable jitter by $\sqrt2$ in variance = +1.5 dB = $\times1.19$ in RMS. From the 85.6-fs model optimum that is $\approx 102$ fs even after re-tuning. derived

Judgment. A 2×-noisier VCO is only partly recoverable by the loop — push $f_c$ up by $\sqrt2$ (to ~3 MHz) and you still eat ~+1.5 dB of jitter, paid for by a 41% larger REF/DTC contribution and a faster, twitchier loop (worse sampled-loop PM erosion, §P8). Widening $f_c$ is the right reflex but it is a damage-limiter, not a fix; if the VCO is permanently 3 dB worse, add a core (§P1) rather than chasing it with bandwidth.

P3 — DTC INL doubles. Quantify the new worst spur and pick the cheapest fix.

GIVEN

Characterization shows the DTC INL is 2× worse than modeled: the dominant 2nd-order term goes from $g_2=0.6$ LSB to $g_2=1.2$ LSB (3rd-order $g_3=0.1\to0.2$ LSB) [A8]. The spur model is $\Delta t = t_{res}(g_2 u^2 + g_3 u^3)$, output spur phase $\varphi = 2\pi f_{out}\,\Delta t$, level $\text{dBc}=20\log_{10}(\varphi_{pk}/2)$ model. At fractional offset $\alpha=0.02$ the baseline worst spur is −56 dBc full-range, −68 dBc ½-range, −92 dBc ⅛-range; NLC cancels it model.

QUESTION

What is the new worst spur at each range setting, and which is cheaper to recover the original −56 dBc full-range spur: more range reduction, or nonlinear calibration (NLC)?

Show worked solution

Step 1 — INL scales the spur linearly in volts → +6 dB. The spur phase is linear in the INL coefficients ($\varphi\propto g_2 u^2+g_3 u^3$), and $\text{dBc}=20\log_{10}(\cdot)$, so doubling the INL adds $20\log_{10}2=+6.0$ dB to every spur. derived

Step 2 — why range reduction is so effective on a 2nd-order INL. Range reduction scales the DTC input swing by $u\to ru$ (factor $r$). The dominant term is $g_2u^2\to g_2 r^2 u^2$, i.e. the 2nd-order spur scales as $r^2$: $20\log_{10}(r^2)=40\log_{10}r$ dB. Halving the range ($r=\tfrac12$) is $40\log_{10}(0.5)=-12$ dB — which is exactly the −56→−68 and −50→−62 steps in the table. derived

Step 3 — recover the original −56 dBc. You are at −50 dBc full-range and want −56 dBc, i.e. need −6 dB. Range reduction gives −12 dB per halving, so a single step to ½-range overshoots the target (lands at −62 dBc, 6 dB better than asked) — and ½-range is already in the architecture (slide 34) at modest cost (DTC range / power $\propto DR^2$, so ½-range cuts that bucket). model slide p34

Step 4 — NLC alternative. NLC drives the modeled INL toward zero (full cancellation in the model), so it would recover far past −56 dBc — but it costs a predistortion engine plus an adaptive loop, and is sensitive to basis conditioning: monomial $[D,D^2,D^3]$ regressors are correlated (ill-conditioned $R$); you must use orthogonal Legendre $P_1,P_2,P_3$ for clean convergence model. That is the right tool when range reduction has run out (e.g. the ⅛-range case still spurs, or you need >−80 dBc), not for a mere 6-dB recovery.

Judgment. For a 6-dB recovery, range reduction wins — flip to the ½-range mode already in the design and you not only recover −56 dBc, you beat it (−62 dBc) for essentially free, while also shrinking the DTC QN floor. Spend NLC's complexity only when you have squeezed range reduction dry and still need deep ($<-80$ dBc) spur suppression.

P4 — Choose the DTC bit-count for a 3rd-order MASH at 6.72 GHz.

GIVEN

You move from the default 2nd-order DSM [A5] to a 3rd-order MASH (MASH-1-1-1). The verified quantization-error range the DTC must absorb is $1/2/4\ T_{vco}$ for MASH-1 / 1-1 / 1-1-1 slide p6. At $f_{out}=6.72$ GHz, $T_{vco}=1/f_{out}=148.8$ ps. Nominal DTC: $T_{res}=400$ fs, $n=10$ bit, $DR=409.6$ ps slide p15. DTC QN floor $\Phi_{DTC,QN}\propto T_{res}^2$ → −163 dBc/Hz at 400 fs slide p15.

QUESTION

What delay range must the DTC cover, how many bits does that take at fixed 400-fs LSB, and is the resulting QN floor still acceptable — or do you need to reduce the LSB (more bits) too?

Show worked solution

Step 1 — required range. MASH-1-1-1 has QE range $4\,T_{vco}$ slide p6:

$$DR_{req}=4\,T_{vco}=4\times148.8\ \text{ps}=595.2\ \text{ps}.$$

The nominal 409.6-ps DTC cannot cover it ($1.25\,T_{vco}$ short). derived

Step 2 — bits at fixed 400-fs LSB. $DR=2^n T_{res}\ge 595.2$ ps with $T_{res}=400$ fs:

$$2^n\ge\frac{595.2\ \text{ps}}{400\ \text{fs}}=1488\;\Rightarrow\;n\ge\lceil\log_2 1488\rceil=11\ \text{bit}\;(2^{11}=2048,\;DR=819.2\ \text{ps}).$$

So 11 bits covers it with margin; 10 bits does not. derived

Step 3 — does the QN floor survive? At fixed $T_{res}=400$ fs the DTC QN floor is unchanged at −163 dBc/Hz — keeping the LSB fixed and only adding the 11th MSB does not raise quantization noise. model Good news: a 3rd-order MASH needs more range, not more resolution. The penalty is range-related power ($\propto DR^2$, so ~$2\times$ the bucket) and one more DTC bit, not a noise penalty.

Step 4 — the catch that pushes back toward range reduction. 3rd-order MASH spreads more high-frequency quantization noise that the DTC must cancel, and the larger $4\,T_{vco}$ swing drives the INL spur up (the $g_2u^2$ term scales with swing², §P3). This is exactly why the modified-DSM ½-range scheme (slide 34) pairs with higher-order MASH: it folds the QE back into $[0,\tfrac12 T_{vco}]$ so the physical DTC range — and its INL spur — shrink even though the DSM order rose. slide p34 model

Judgment. Going 2nd→3rd-order MASH is a range problem, not a resolution problem: add one DTC bit (10→11) to span $4\,T_{vco}=595$ ps, keep the 400-fs LSB so the −163 dBc/Hz QN floor and in-band jitter are untouched. But the larger swing worsens INL spurs and DTC power, so pair the higher-order MASH with the modified-DSM ½-range trick — that is the whole reason the two appear together in the deck.

P5 — The $K_{DTC}$ gain calibration settles to 0.5% error. What in-band floor, and is the cal fast enough?

GIVEN

The background $K_{DTC}$ gain cal (sign-error LMS, slide 35/37) settles with residual gain error $\varepsilon=0.5\%=5\times10^{-3}$. The verified residual-floor model: a leftover gain error $\varepsilon$ leaves an in-band floor $S=\varepsilon^2(2\pi)^2 S_{Q,dsm}|H_{ref}|^2$, integrating to 4.5 fs at $\varepsilon=10^{-4}$, 45 fs at $10^{-3}$, 449 fs at $10^{-2}$ — i.e. floor $\propto\varepsilon$, and you need $\varepsilon\lesssim2\times10^{-4}$ for $<10$ fs model. Convergence target $\approx30$ µs [A17]; $f_{ref}=104$ MHz → ~3120 ref cycles in 30 µs.

QUESTION

How much jitter does 0.5% residual gain error inject, where does it sit relative to the 87.6-fs budget, and is the −30-µs convergence assumption compatible with that residual?

Show worked solution

Step 1 — floor at $\varepsilon=0.5\%$. The floor is linear in $\varepsilon$ (since $S\propto\varepsilon^2$ in power → $\sigma_t\propto\varepsilon$). Interpolate between the verified anchors $45$ fs @ $10^{-3}$ and $449$ fs @ $10^{-2}$:

$$\sigma_{t,\varepsilon}=45\ \text{fs}\times\frac{5\times10^{-3}}{1\times10^{-3}}=225\ \text{fs}.$$

(Check the other anchor: $449\times\tfrac{5\times10^{-3}}{10^{-2}}=224.5$ fs — consistent.) derived

Step 2 — compare to budget. 225 fs alone is 2.6× the entire 87.6-fs budget. Added in quadrature: $\sqrt{87.6^2+225^2}=241$ fs — the residual dominates and blows the spec by ~2.7×. A 0.5% gain cal is nowhere near good enough; you need $\varepsilon\lesssim2\times10^{-4}$ ($<10$ fs) to keep the cal residual a non-player. model

Step 3 — convergence vs. residual: the misadjustment trade. Sign-LMS steady-state ripple (misadjustment) $\propto\mu$ model, while convergence time $\propto1/\mu$. So tightening $\varepsilon$ from $5\times10^{-3}$ down to $2\times10^{-4}$ (a 25× smaller residual) means a ~25× smaller $\mu$ → ~25× longer settling. If 0.5% took the budgeted 30 µs, then $2\times10^{-4}$ would take on the order of 0.75 ms at fixed $\mu$. That breaks the −30-µs assumption [A17]. derived

Step 4 — the resolution: gear-shift LMS. You cannot get both fast lock and tiny residual with one $\mu$. Use a two-phase (gear-shifted) µ: large $\mu$ for the first ~30 µs to pull $K_{DTC}$ into the basin quickly, then drop $\mu$ by ~25× (or freeze and dither) so the steady-state residual lands at $\varepsilon\sim2\times10^{-4}$. This decouples the two requirements (see P8 for the same trick on lock-time vs. misadjustment). model

Judgment. 0.5% residual is a disqualifying 225 fs — the gain cal must reach $\varepsilon\lesssim2\times10^{-4}$, two-plus decades tighter, before the floor disappears under the budget. A single LMS step size cannot serve both the 30-µs lock and that residual; gear-shift $\mu$ (big then small) is the standard answer, and it is the same lever P8 uses for lock-time vs. misadjustment.

P6 — Reference doubler on or off? Trade DTC range against the CKREF duty spur.

GIVEN

The chip has an optional reference doubler $\text{CKREF}\to\times2\to\text{CKREFX2}\to\text{DTC}$ slide p39 (see open_questions.md #6). Doubling pushes the effective $f_{ref}$ from 104→208 MHz, which halves the per-cycle fractional slip the DTC must span (smaller DTC range). But a doubler made from a delay-XOR has duty-cycle error, and the verified spur model shows a duty error injects an every-other-cycle tone: VCO 20-ps duty error → −64 dBc cal-off, cancelled cal-on model. The CKREF duty error injected in the cal demos is 1 ns (57%) [A18] slide p40.

QUESTION

What does turning the doubler on buy you in DTC range/QN, what spur does it threaten, and what must be true (calibration-wise) for "doubler on" to be the right call?

Show worked solution

Step 1 — the win: smaller DTC range. The DTC must absorb the accumulated DSM QE per reference edge. Doubling $f_{ref}$ halves the time between edges, so the per-cycle phase slip — and the required DTC delay range — drops by ~2×. Since DTC power $\propto DR^2$ slide p33, halving $DR$ cuts DTC range-power ~4×, and at fixed bits it doubles effective resolution (lower LSB → lower QN, $\propto T_{res}^2$). The DTC bucket shrinks. model

Step 2 — the threat: duty-cycle spur at $f_{ref}/2$. A doubler with duty error puts unequal half-periods → an every-other-cycle timing error, which is exactly the duty-spur mechanism in the model ($\Delta t$ alternates $\pm$ per cycle, tone at $f_{ref}/2$). The model quantifies the analogous VCO 20-ps duty error at −64 dBc cal-off model; a CKREF-side doubler with comparable duty error lands in the same ballpark and would dominate the spur mask unless cancelled.

Step 3 — what makes "doubler on" safe. The verified result is that the duty spur is cancelled cal-on — the CKREF-DCC / VCO-DCC sign-sign LMS loops drive the duty error out using a $\pm1$ "every-other-cycle" regressor (XOR / up-down counter) model slide p40. So doubler-on is the right call iff the duty-cycle correction loop is present and converged: with DCC on, the −64-dBc-class spur is cancelled and you keep the range/QN win for free. Without a working DCC, the duty spur (−64 dBc cal-off, vs. measured frac spurs of −70…−85 dBc slide p41) breaks the spur mask — doubler off is then safer.

Judgment. Turn the doubler on if and only if the duty-cycle correction (DCC) loop is implemented and converged — then you bank a 2× smaller DTC range (≈4× less range-power, lower QN) and the −64-dBc-class duty spur is cancelled to below the −70…−85-dBc measured spur floor. If there is any doubt the DCC will track over PVT, leave it off: an uncancelled −64-dBc duty tone is worse than carrying the larger DTC.

P7 — Pick $\mu$ for 30-µs lock and <1% misadjustment. Can you do both with one step size?

GIVEN

Sign-LMS background cal: steady-state ripple (misadjustment) $\propto\mu$ model, convergence time $\tau\propto1/\mu$. Spec: lock within 30 µs [A17] (~3120 ref cycles at 104 MHz) and steady-state misadjustment <1% of the converged value. The sign_lms_ripple(µ) model shows ripple growing monotonically with $\mu$ over $\mu\in[0.05,6]$ model.

QUESTION

Is there a single $\mu$ that meets both? If not, what is the standard fix and how do you schedule it?

Show worked solution

Step 1 — the two requirements pull opposite ways. Fast lock wants large $\mu$ ($\tau\propto1/\mu$); low misadjustment wants small $\mu$ (ripple $\propto\mu$). Their product is a constant figure of merit: $\tau\times(\text{misadjustment})\approx\text{const}$. model

Step 2 — is the single-$\mu$ window non-empty? Suppose the $\mu$ that gives 30-µs lock produces misadjustment $M_{30}$. To also hit <1% you would need $\mu$ small enough that $M\propto\mu$ falls below 1%. If $M_{30}$ already exceeds 1% (the usual case for a one-bit sign-LMS pulled to lock in only ~3120 cycles, where ripple is several percent model), then no single $\mu$ works: lowering $\mu$ to fix misadjustment stretches $\tau$ past 30 µs, and raising $\mu$ to hit 30 µs blows the 1% ripple. The constant-product law says you cannot beat the trade with one knob. derived

Step 3 — the fix: gear-shifted (two-stage) $\mu$. Decouple the two phases:

  1. Acquire (0–30 µs): large $\mu_{hi}$ sized for $\tau\approx5$–8 µs so the loop is within a few LSB of $K_{DTC}$ by 30 µs (slide-37 "<30 µs" slide p37). Misadjustment is high here but you do not care yet.
  2. Track (after lock): drop to $\mu_{lo}=\mu_{hi}/k$. Since ripple $\propto\mu$, choosing $k$ so that $\mu_{lo}$ gives <1% sets the steady-state residual. If $\mu_{hi}$ gave, say, ~10% ripple, then $k\approx10$–20 (drop one decade-ish) lands <1%.

This is the same gear-shift used in P5 to reconcile 30-µs convergence with $\varepsilon\le2\times10^{-4}$ residual gain error — one mechanism, two specs. model

Step 4 — bonus: why the co-running cals do not fight during the shift. The regressors $\Phi_e$, $\text{SEL\_CKFB}(\pm1)$, $\text{even\_cycle}(\pm1)$, $1$ (offset) are mutually orthogonal under expectation model, so you can gear-shift each loop independently — except half-range $\Phi_e$ has nonzero mean, which couples offset into gain. So schedule the offset cal to converge first, then gear-shift the gain cal; otherwise the offset leakage corrupts the high-$\mu$ acquire of $K_{DTC}$. model

Judgment. No single $\mu$ meets both — the ripple-vs-speed product is a hard constant, so the two specs live on opposite ends of one knob. Gear-shift: big $\mu$ for ~30-µs acquire, then drop ~1–1.5 decades for <1% tracking misadjustment, and converge the offset cal first because half-range $\Phi_e$ leaks offset into the gain loop.

P8 — Someone proposes $f_c = f_{ref}/8$ to kill VCO noise. Where does the sampled loop bite back?

GIVEN

An aggressive reviewer says: "the VCO owns 51% of the budget — just crank $f_c$ way up to $f_{ref}/8 = 13$ MHz and high-pass the VCO into oblivion." The present design sits at $f_c=1.5$ MHz, i.e. $f_c/f_{ref}\approx1/70$, where the verified note is that sampled-loop PM erosion is small because $f_c/f_{ref}\approx1/70$, with a zero-order-hold half-sample lag $\approx-\pi f/f_{ref}$ model. The 2nd-order approx already shows gain-peaking 1.70 dB, ~25% phase-step overshoot, and a zero at $f_z=402$ kHz driving the overshoot model; jitter-optimal $f_c^\*\approx2.1$ MHz model.

QUESTION

Estimate the extra phase lag the sampling introduces at $f_c=13$ MHz, decide whether the loop is still stable, and say what actually limits how far you can push $f_c$.

Show worked solution

Step 1 — sampled-loop lag at the present design. The ZOH half-sample lag is $\Delta\phi\approx-\pi f/f_{ref}$ model. At $f_c=1.5$ MHz:

$$\Delta\phi=-\pi\frac{1.5}{104}=-0.0453\ \text{rad}=-2.6^\circ.$$

Negligible against the 60° PM [A2] — which is exactly why the deck's slow loop ignores it. derived

Step 2 — lag at the proposed $f_c=f_{ref}/8$. Now $f/f_{ref}=1/8$:

$$\Delta\phi=-\pi\cdot\tfrac18=-0.393\ \text{rad}=-22.5^\circ.$$

The sampling alone eats 22.5° of phase. Subtract it from the 60° target and the loop has only ~37.5° PM — before accounting for the extra delay of the SPD/DTC datapath and any anti-alias pole. The 1.70-dB gain peaking model gets dramatically worse as PM collapses, and overshoot/ringing balloon past the 25% baseline. derived

Step 3 — is it even worth it? The jitter optimum is at $f_c^\*\approx2.1$ MHz model; pushing past it makes total jitter worse, not better, because beyond the optimum the rising REF+DTC contribution (each $\times N^2$) overtakes the falling VCO contribution (P2's $\sigma^2\approx A/f_c+Bf_c$ has a minimum, not a monotone). So $f_c=13$ MHz is on the wrong side of the optimum and stability-eroded. model

Step 4 — what really limits $f_c$. Three ceilings, in order of bite: (1) the jitter optimum at ~2.1 MHz (going past it raises jitter); (2) sampled-loop stability — the $-\pi f/f_{ref}$ lag is tolerable to maybe $f_c\lesssim f_{ref}/20$ (≈5 MHz, ~9° lag) before PM erosion forces redesign; (3) the classic Gardner rule of thumb $f_c\lesssim f_{ref}/10$ for a clean continuous-time approximation textbook (Gardner). All three say "low single-digit MHz," not 13 MHz.

The reviewer's "just crank $f_c$" fails on two fronts: the jitter already bottoms at ~2.1 MHz (13 MHz is past the optimum, so it raises jitter), and the sampled-loop ZOH lag grows as $\pi f/f_{ref}$ — harmless at 1.5 MHz (−2.6°) but a PM-destroying −22.5° at $f_{ref}/8$. Keep $f_c$ in the low-single-digit MHz; to beat the VCO line, add cores (P1), do not chase it with bandwidth.

Self-check quiz. Quick sanity tests on the judgment patterns above.
Q1. A teammate says "drive the SPD+GM noise (4% of budget) to zero and we'll meet a 60-fs spec." Without computing, why is this hopeless?
Show answer
Because the VCO alone owns 51% of the variance. Even zeroing everything except the VCO leaves $\sigma_t=87.6\sqrt{0.51}=62.6$ fs > 60 fs — and SPD+GM is only 4%, so deleting it moves the total by $\sqrt{1-0.04}=0.98$, i.e. ~88→86 fs. You must attack the dominant (VCO) line; small lines round to nothing in quadrature. slide p42 derived (see P1)
Q2. The DTC INL doubles. Your colleague wants to add an NLC engine immediately. Give the one-line reason range reduction is the cheaper first move for a 6-dB recovery.
Show answer
Doubling INL adds only $20\log_{10}2=+6$ dB to the spur, and range reduction removes $40\log_{10}r$ dB (= −12 dB per halving) on the dominant 2nd-order term. One halving to ½-range — already in the architecture — overshoots the 6-dB target (lands −62 dBc) for free, no new engine. Save NLC for when you need >−80 dBc. model (see P3)
Q3. Why can't one LMS step size $\mu$ give both 30-µs lock and <1% misadjustment, and what is the standard escape?
Show answer
Convergence $\tau\propto1/\mu$ but misadjustment ripple $\propto\mu$, so $\tau\times M$ is a fixed product — fast lock and low ripple sit on opposite ends of the same knob. Escape: gear-shift $\mu$ (large to acquire in ~30 µs, then drop ~1–1.5 decades to track at <1%). Converge the offset cal first, since half-range $\Phi_e$ has nonzero mean and leaks offset into the gain loop. model (see P5, P7)
