1. Why a frequency-domain model at all?

A PLL is a feedback loop, and feedback loops are easiest to reason about in the frequency domain. The phase-domain block diagram on slide p5 is the canonical linear model textbook (Razavi/Gardner): a phase detector, a loop filter, a VCO modeled as an integrator derived, and a feedback divider $1/N$. Two questions follow immediately:

  • Stability & speed. How fast does the loop respond, and does it ring? → the open-loop gain $G_\text{ol}(s)$, its crossover $f_c$, and its phase margin (PM).
  • Noise. Every block injects noise at a different node. The loop low-passes some sources and high-passes others. → the closed-loop noise transfer functions (NTFs) and the integrated jitter budget.

2. Open-loop gain $G_\text{ol}(s)$ — a type-II loop

2.1 The model

The proposed loop has two poles at the origin: one from the VCO (control voltage → phase is an integration, $K_\text{VCO}/s$) and one from the integral path of the loop filter (the $V_\text{ctrl,I}$ path on slide p39). Two integrators give $-180^\circ$ of phase at all frequencies, which would be marginally stable — so a zero (the proportional $V_\text{ctrl,P}$ path) is added to pull the phase back up near crossover, and a high-frequency pole cleans up ripple. That is a textbook type-II second-order loop textbook (Gardner, "Phaselock Techniques"). The model in code uses the compact form derived:

$$ G_\text{ol}(s) \;=\; K_\text{loop}\,\frac{1 + s/\omega_z}{s^{2}\,\bigl(1 + s/\omega_p\bigr)} $$

The $s^2$ in the denominator is the two origin poles; $\omega_z$ is the stabilizing zero; $\omega_p$ is the HF pole. Forward gain is $K_\text{PD}\,H_\text{LF}(s)\,K_\text{VCO}/s$ and the open loop folds in the $1/N$ feedback derived (derivations §1): $G_\text{ol}(s) = K_\text{PD}\,H_\text{LF}(s)\,(K_\text{VCO}/s)\,(1/N)$.

2.2 The symmetric design recipe (f_c, PM → zero & pole)

We do not pick $\omega_z$, $\omega_p$, $K_\text{loop}$ by hand; we solve for them from the two design targets. Place the zero and pole symmetrically (in log-frequency) about the crossover $\omega_c = 2\pi f_c$. For a symmetric type-II loop the phase margin is $\text{PM} = 2\arctan(k) - 90^\circ$ derived, which inverts to:

$$ k \;=\; \tan\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\text{PM} + 90^\circ}{2}\Bigr),\qquad \omega_z = \frac{\omega_c}{k},\qquad \omega_p = \omega_c\, k . $$

Then the gain constant $K_\text{loop}$ is fixed by forcing $|G_\text{ol}(j\omega_c)| = 1$ (unity gain at crossover). With $f_c = 1.5$ MHz and PM $= 60^\circ$ this gives $k = \tan(75^\circ) \approx 3.73$, so (from freq_domain.json):

$\omega_z = 2.525\times10^{6}$ rad/s → 402 kHz; $\omega_p = 3.517\times10^{7}$ rad/s → 5.60 MHz; $K_\text{loop} = 2.38\times10^{13}$ — all derived in PLLParams._design_type2_loop(). $K_\text{VCO}$ is assumption [A4].

Why symmetric placement? The phase of $G_\text{ol}$ at $\omega_c$ is maximized when $\omega_c = \sqrt{\omega_z\,\omega_p}$ textbook (Gardner). Putting the zero a factor $k$ below $f_c$ and the pole a factor $k$ above $f_c$ gives exactly that geometric-mean crossover, so the chosen PM is the maximum phase margin for that zero/pole spacing — the most robust design for a given bandwidth. Check: $\sqrt{402\text{ kHz}\cdot 5.60\text{ MHz}} \approx 1.50$ MHz $= f_c$. ✓
Worked example — designing the loop from two targets. The §2.2 recipe is just two formulas; here is every digit.

GIVEN. $f_c = 1.5$ MHz assumption [A1], $\text{PM}=60^\circ$ assumption [A2]. So $\omega_c = 2\pi f_c = 9.4248\times10^{6}$ rad/s.

STEP 1 — spacing factor. Invert $\text{PM}=2\arctan(k)-90^\circ$:

$$ k=\tan\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\text{PM}+90^\circ}{2}\Bigr)=\tan\!\Bigl(\tfrac{60^\circ+90^\circ}{2}\Bigr)=\tan(75^\circ)=3.7321. $$

STEP 2 — zero and pole, symmetric about $f_c$.

$$ f_z=\frac{f_c}{k}=\frac{1.5\text{ MHz}}{3.7321}=401.9\text{ kHz},\qquad f_p=f_c\,k=1.5\text{ MHz}\times3.7321=5.598\text{ MHz}. $$

(i.e. $\omega_z=2.5254\times10^{6}$, $\omega_p=3.5174\times10^{7}$ rad/s.)

STEP 3 — gain constant from unity-gain-at-crossover. Demand $|G_\text{ol}(j\omega_c)|=1$. The pure shape factor evaluated at $j\omega_c$ is

$$ \Bigl|\tfrac{1+j\omega_c/\omega_z}{(j\omega_c)^2\,(1+j\omega_c/\omega_p)}\Bigr| = 4.2015\times10^{-14},\qquad K_\text{loop}=\frac{1}{4.2015\times10^{-14}}=2.380\times10^{13}. $$

SANITY CHECK. Geometric-mean crossover $\sqrt{f_z f_p}=\sqrt{401.9\text{ kHz}\times5.598\text{ MHz}}=1.500$ MHz $=f_c$ ✓, and plugging back gives $|G_\text{ol}(j\omega_c)|=1.000$ with $\angle G_\text{ol}=-120^\circ$, so $\text{PM}=180^\circ-120^\circ=60^\circ$ ✓ — matching the numerically-extracted $f_c=1.501$ MHz / PM $=59.99^\circ$ in §6 model.

3. Closed-loop noise transfer functions (NTFs)

Close the loop and two master transfer functions appear. With $G \equiv G_\text{ol}(s)$ derived (derivations §1) textbook:

Reference → output (low-pass)

$$ H_\text{ref}(s) = \frac{\Phi_\text{out}}{\Phi_\text{ref}} = \frac{N\,G}{1+G} $$
  • LF ($f\!\to\!0$, $G\!\to\!\infty$): $H_\text{ref}\to N$. The output phase tracks the reference, scaled by the divide ratio. DC gain = $N = 64.62$.
  • HF ($f\!\gg\!f_c$, $G\!\to\!0$): $H_\text{ref}\to 0$. The loop cannot follow fast reference jitter — it is low-passed at $f_c$.

Anything that enters with the reference (CKREF noise, DTC QN/thermal, calibration residual) shares this LP shape.

Worked example — why $H_\text{ref}(0)=N$ (a one-line limit, with the number).

GIVEN. $H_\text{ref}(s)=N\,G/(1+G)$ with $N=f_\text{out}/f_\text{ref}=6.72\text{ GHz}/104\text{ MHz}=64.615$ derived, and $G\equiv G_\text{ol}(s)$ the type-II open loop (two poles at the origin).

STEP 1 — how big is $G$ at low offset? The $s^{-2}$ from the two integrators makes $G$ huge in band. Evaluating the model:

$$ |G_\text{ol}(j2\pi\cdot100\text{ Hz})|=6.03\times10^{7},\qquad |G_\text{ol}(j2\pi\cdot1\text{ kHz})|=6.03\times10^{5}. $$

STEP 2 — take the limit. With $G\gg1$, $\dfrac{G}{1+G}\to1$, so

$$ H_\text{ref}(0)=N\cdot\lim_{G\to\infty}\frac{G}{1+G}=N\cdot1=64.615. $$

SANITY CHECK. The model returns $|H_\text{ref}|=64.6154$ at 1, 10, 100, and 1000 Hz — flat to 5 digits and equal to $N$ model, exactly the §6 check “$H_\text{ref}(0)\to64.62$, pass.” Physically: in band the loop forces the output phase to track the reference, multiplied up by the divide ratio. By the same algebra $H_\text{vco}=1/(1+G)\to1/(6\times10^{7})\approx0$ at DC — the loop scrubs VCO noise inside $f_c$.

VCO → output (high-pass)

$$ H_\text{vco}(s) = \frac{\Phi_\text{out}}{\Phi_\text{vco}} = \frac{1}{1+G} $$
  • LF ($G\!\to\!\infty$): $H_\text{vco}\to 0$. Inside the loop bandwidth the feedback suppresses VCO noise — the loop "cleans up" the oscillator.
  • HF ($G\!\to\!0$): $H_\text{vco}\to 1$. Outside the bandwidth the VCO runs free; its own phase noise passes straight to the output unattenuated.

This is the classic trade: widen $f_c$ to suppress more VCO noise, but then more reference/DTC noise leaks in. The 1.5 MHz BW is the chosen compromise.

Reading the table. There are really only three shapes. Everything injected with the reference or at the PD/divider input shares the low-pass $N\,G/(1+G)$ shape (it competes with the reference and is tracked inside $f_c$). The VCO is the lone high-pass $1/(1+G)$ source. The loop-filter / post-PD noise is band-pass — high-passed by the integrator it has to drive, low-passed by the loop. Knowing the shape tells you where each source matters before you compute a single number.
The DSM quantization noise and its cancellation. A MASH-$m$ modulator produces white quantization noise at the divider input shaped by $(1-z^{-1})^m$, with PSD (single-sided, cycles²/Hz) textbook (Riley, JSSC'93): $$ S_{Q_\text{DSM}}(f) = \frac{1}{12}\,\frac{1}{f_\text{ref}}\,\bigl[2\sin(\pi f/f_\text{ref})\bigr]^{2m}. $$ Referred to output phase it is multiplied by $(2\pi)^2$ (cycles → rad) and passes through the loop's low-pass — giving the classic noise "hump" near $f_c$. The whole point of the DTC is to cancel the in-band accumulated quantization error slide p9: the DTC pre-distorts the reference edge by the DSM's known accumulated phase, so the residual is pushed to $\approx 0$ (slide 42 lists $\Sigma\Delta$M QE $\approx 0\%$). In the model this is an $\times 10^{-8}$ ($-80$ dB) residual, leaving only $0.3\%$ of the variance.

5. Jitter budget — reproducing slide 42

To get a single number we (a) multiply each source's input PSD by $|{\rm NTF}|^2$ to refer it to the output, (b) integrate over the band $1$ kHz – $100$ MHz to get a variance $\sigma_\phi^2$, and (c) convert to RMS jitter $\sigma_t = \sigma_\phi/(2\pi f_\text{out})$ textbook (derivations §8). 5.1 Where the DTC numbers come from

The DTC contributes via two white floors injected on the reference path slides p15, p16:

  • Quantization noise. $\mathcal{L}_\text{DTC,QN} = \dfrac{(2\pi\,T_\text{res})^2}{12}\,f_\text{ref}$ with $T_\text{res} = 400$ fs and $f_\text{ref} = 104$ MHz gives $-162.6$ dBc/Hz derived (derivations §3); the slide rounds to $-163$ dBc/Hz.
  • Thermal floor. $-171$ dBc/Hz slide p16, set by the sampled $kT/C$ converted to timing through the slew rate.

Both are low-passed by $H_\text{ref}$, so they only matter inside $f_c$.

Worked example — building the DTC row of the budget number-by-number.

GIVEN. Two flat floors injected on the reference path: DTC QN $=-163$ dBc/Hz slide p15 and DTC thermal $=-171$ dBc/Hz slide p16. Band $1\text{ kHz}\!-\!100\text{ MHz}$ assumption [A16]. $f_\text{out}=6.72$ GHz, $N=64.615$.

STEP 1 — dBc/Hz → phase PSD (with $\mathcal{L}=10\log_{10}(\tfrac12 S_\phi)$, so $S_\phi=2\cdot10^{\mathcal{L}/10}$) textbook (IEEE-1139):

$$ S_{\phi,\text{QN}}=2\cdot10^{-16.3}=1.002\times10^{-16},\quad S_{\phi,\text{th}}=2\cdot10^{-17.1}=1.589\times10^{-17}, $$ $$ S_{\phi,\text{DTC}}=1.161\times10^{-16}\ \text{rad}^2/\text{Hz (flat)}. $$

STEP 2 — refer to output and integrate. The DTC sees the low-pass $H_\text{ref}$, so $\sigma_\phi^2=\int S_{\phi,\text{DTC}}\,|H_\text{ref}|^2\,df = S_{\phi,\text{DTC}}\,N^2\!\int|H_\text{ref}/N|^2\,df$. The shape integral is an effective noise bandwidth $\text{ENBW}=3.218$ MHz $(\approx2.14\,f_c)$ model:

$$ \sigma_\phi^2 = (1.161\times10^{-16})\,(64.615^2)\,(3.218\times10^{6})=1.560\times10^{-6}\ \text{rad}^2, $$ $$ \sigma_\phi=1.249\times10^{-3}\ \text{rad}. $$

STEP 3 — convert to jitter and to dBc:

$$ \sigma_t=\frac{\sigma_\phi}{2\pi f_\text{out}}=\frac{1.249\times10^{-3}}{2\pi\cdot6.72\times10^{9}}=29.6\ \text{fs},\qquad \text{IPN}=10\log_{10}\!\bigl(\tfrac12\sigma_\phi^2\bigr)=-61.1\ \text{dBc}. $$

SANITY CHECK. This is exactly the DTC row of the §5 table (29.58 fs, 11.4% of variance) model. Round-trip: $2\pi f_\text{out}\sigma_t=2\pi\cdot6.72\text{ GHz}\cdot29.58\text{ fs}=1.249\times10^{-3}$ rad recovers $\sigma_\phi$ ✓. Every other low-pass row (REF, MMD, SPD+GM) is the same three steps with its own flat floor; the VCO row uses $|H_\text{vco}|^2$ on a sloped PSD instead.

This is why DR (dynamic range) reduction matters: $\text{DTC QN}\propto T_\text{res}^2$ and $T_\text{res}\propto \text{DR}$, so the ½- and 1/8-range techniques (slides 34, 38) cut this contribution directly slide p33 derived (derivations §5).

Open ambiguity (carried from derivations §3, §8): the SSB / DSB factor of 2. Whether a flat quantization PSD is spread over $f_\text{ref}$ (double-sided) or $f_\text{ref}/2$ (single-sided), and whether IPN is quoted SSB or DSB, is a 3 dB choice. We adopt the slide's convention ($(2\pi T_\text{res})^2/12\cdot f_\text{ref}$, which reproduces $-163$ dBc/Hz) and report IPN as SSB. Slide 42's "$\approx$87.5 fs / $-51.7$ dBc" is consistent with our 87.6 fs / $-51.6$ dBc within exactly this 3 dB ambiguity. needs citation for the exact convention.

Self-check

Q1. The open-loop gain is written $G_\text{ol}(s) = K_\text{loop}\,(1+s/\omega_z)/[s^2(1+s/\omega_p)]$. What makes this a type-II loop, and where do the two integrations physically come from?
Show answer
Type-II means there are two poles at the origin — the $s^2$ in the denominator. One integration comes from the VCO (control voltage → phase is $K_\text{VCO}/s$) and the other from the integral path of the loop filter (the $V_\text{ctrl,I}$ path). Two integrators give $-180^\circ$ of phase, so a stabilizing zero $\omega_z$ (the proportional $V_\text{ctrl,P}$ path) is added to recover phase margin. slide p39 derived
Q2. As $f\!\to\!0$, what does $H_\text{ref}(s)=NG/(1+G)$ approach, and what does $H_\text{vco}(s)=1/(1+G)$ approach? Give the actual DC value of $H_\text{ref}$.
Show answer
At DC, $G\!\to\!\infty$, so $H_\text{ref}\to N = 64.62$ — the output phase tracks the reference scaled by the divide ratio (low-pass, $\times N$). Meanwhile $H_\text{vco}\to 0$: inside the loop bandwidth the feedback suppresses VCO noise. At high frequency the roles flip ($H_\text{ref}\to 0$ low-pass, $H_\text{vco}\to 1$ high-pass). DC gain $N=64.62$ is confirmed by the sanity check $H_\text{ref}(0)\to 64.62$. derived (derivations §1) slide p5
Q3. In the 87.6 fs budget, which source dominates and why does the loop bandwidth $f_c$ set up a REF-vs-VCO trade-off?
Show answer
The VCO dominates at 51.8% of the variance (63.06 fs of the 87.60 fs total), because it is the lone high-pass source ($H_\text{vco}=1/(1+G)\to 1$ above $f_c$) and runs free outside the loop bandwidth. Widening $f_c$ suppresses more VCO noise (lowers the 51.8% share), but the low-pass REF+DTC sources (37.8%) then leak in more — so $f_c = 1.5$ MHz is the chosen compromise. The fs numbers add in quadrature: $\sqrt{63.06^2+44.99^2+\dots}=87.6$ fs. slide p42 model
