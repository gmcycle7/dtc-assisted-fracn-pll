Calibrations — the four background LMS loops that make the DTC honest

A high-performance DTC-assisted fractional-N PLL only works if the DTC gain, the VCO and reference duty cycles, and the comparator/GM offset are all continuously trimmed by sign-LMS loops that converge in under 30 µs and track PVT in the background.

0. Why calibrate at all — the big picture

In the proposed analog sampling PLL slide p9, a Digital-to-Time Converter (DTC) sits in the reference path and adds a programmable delay slide p9

$$\Delta t[n] \;=\; \frac{1}{K_{\rm DTC}}\,\Phi_{\rm QE}[n], \qquad K_{\rm DTC} = \frac{T_{\rm vco}}{\Delta t_{\rm DTC}}\ \ \text{[codes per VCO period]}$$

that cancels the accumulated ΔΣ quantization error slide p9. When the cancellation is perfect, the phase detector sees ≈0 phase error — the fractional-N PLL behaves like an integer-N PLL. But the cancellation is only as good as four physical parameters, every one of which drifts with process, voltage and temperature. So we run a stack of digital background calibrations slide p40:

  • A. DTC gain \(K_{\rm DTC}\): if the gain is wrong the cancellation leaves a residual ∝ \(\Phi_e\) → in-band noise / fractional spur. slides p26–28, p40
  • B. VCO duty-cycle (vco_dcc): the ½-range trick uses two VCO phases that must be exactly \(T_{\rm vco}/2\) apart; duty error breaks that. slides p35–37, p40
  • C. CKREF duty-cycle (ckref_dcc): a non-50% reference clock creates an alternating-cycle period error. slide p40
  • D. Comparator/GM offset (Vref_adj): a DC offset biases the 1-bit error mean, which corrupts all the sign-LMS loops above. slides p27–28, p40

Plus one survey-only technique we model for completeness:

  • E. Polynomial DTC nonlinearity correction (\(g_1,g_2,g_3\)) — three parallel LMS loops that pre-distort the DTC code. slide p31 (survey, [Park '21])
One shared engine. All four proposed loops are the same algorithm: a sign-(sign-)LMS integrator textbook (Widrow & Stearns, Adaptive Signal Processing). They all share the single 1-bit error \(e[k]=\operatorname{sign}(\text{PHE})\) from the comparator and differ only in their regressor \(x[k]\) (what they correlate the error against). Learn the engine once and you understand all four. derived (derivations.md §7)
$$\boxed{\;w[k+1] = w[k] + \mu\,\cdot e[k]\cdot x[k]\;}\qquad\text{generic LMS, integrator } \tfrac{\mu z^{-1}}{1-z^{-1}}$$

slide p26 textbook

A. DTC gain calibration (\(K_{\rm DTC}\)) — sign-error LMS

(1) Purpose / what error it fixes

The DTC must apply a delay \(\Delta t[n]=\Phi_{\rm QE}[n]/K_{\rm DTC}\) that exactly cancels the accumulated DSM quantization error slide p9. If the actual gain \(\hat K\) differs from the true \(K_{\rm true}\) (because \(\Delta t_{\rm DTC}=T_{\rm vco}/K_{\rm DTC}\) drifts with PVT), the cancellation is imperfect and leaves a residual phase error proportional to \(\Phi_e\) slide p27. Since \(\Phi_e\) is correlated with the fractional sequence, this residual reappears as in-band quantization noise and a fractional spur — exactly what the DTC was supposed to remove.

(2) Signal path

Observable
Phase-detector error PHE (sampled voltage out of the SPD/GM, sliced to 1 bit by the comparator). slide p27
Actuator
The DTC code word: main term DTC_code = K_DTC·Φe[n]. slide p40
Target
\(\hat K \to K_{\rm true}\) so the residual ∝ \((K_{\rm true}-\hat K)\Phi_e \to 0\). derived
Error signal
\(e[k]=\operatorname{sign}(\text{PHE})\) — a 1-bit comparator output. slide p27
Regressor
\(x[k]=\Phi_e[k-n]\) (the accumulated DSM phase the DTC was correcting). slide p40
Update law
\(\hat K[k{+}1]=\hat K[k]+\mu_2\,e[k]\,\Phi_e[k{-}n]\). slide p40

(3) Mathematical model

This is classic LMS system identification textbook (Widrow & Stearns): the "plant" is the map from the regressor \(\Phi_e\) to the residual error, and the adaptive weight is \(K_{\rm DTC}\). slide p26 The residual and update are derived (derivations.md §7.1):

$$\text{PHE}[k] \;=\; \frac{K_{\rm true}-\hat K[k]}{K_{\rm true}}\,\Phi_e[k] \;+\; \text{offset} \;+\; \text{noise}$$ $$e[k]=\operatorname{sign}\!\big(\text{PHE}[k]\big),\qquad \hat K[k{+}1]=\hat K[k]+\mu_2\,e[k]\,\Phi_e[k]$$

Because the comparator slices to 1 bit, this is sign-error LMS (the error is 1-bit, the regressor \(\Phi_e\) is full precision).

(4) Convergence analysis

Mean recursion & stability bound. Taking expectations (and assuming \(e\) is zero-mean), \(E\{e\cdot\Phi_e\}\propto (K_{\rm true}-\hat K)\,E\{\Phi_e^2\}\), so \(\hat K\) follows a first-order recursion derived:

$$\tau \;\approx\; \frac{1}{\mu_2\,E\{\Phi_e^2\}\,\eta\,(2f_{\rm ref})},\qquad \text{converges iff } 0<\mu_2<\frac{2}{E\{\Phi_e^2\}}$$

where \(\eta=\sqrt{2/\pi}/\sigma\) is the sign-LMS slope factor for Gaussian residual noise textbook.

  • Step-size trade-off. Small \(\mu\) → slow but quiet; large \(\mu\) → fast but overshoots and dithers; too large → instability (violates the bound). See cal_stepsize.png.
  • Limit cycle. A 1-bit error never reaches exactly zero — the converged \(\hat K\) dithers by ±\(\mu\) around the truth. The useful output is the smoothed/averaged value (that is why _settle_time measures settling on a moving average).
  • Offset bias (the headline coupling). If \(e[k]\) has a nonzero mean \(m\) (from comparator/GM offset), the update picks up a bias term \(\mu_2\,m\,E\{\Phi_e\}\) and \(\hat K\) converges to a wrong value slide p27. With \(\Phi_e\in[0,0.5]\) (½-range, so \(E\{\Phi_e\}\neq0\)) this is severe — see cal D.
  • Foreground vs background. Run continuously after lock (background) so it tracks PVT slide p40 assumption [A20]; a foreground version would freeze \(\hat K\) after an initial training burst.

B. VCO duty-cycle calibration (vco_dcc) — sign-LMS

(1) Purpose / what error it fixes

The proposed ½-range technique slide p34 halves the DTC dynamic range by selecting between two VCO phases, CKFB1 and CKFB2 = CKFB1 + ½T_vco, via SEL_CKFB. This only works if the two phases are exactly \(T_{\rm vco}/2\) apart. If the VCO duty cycle ≠ 50%, they are not slide p35:

$$\Delta t_{\rm err} = \Delta t - \frac{T_{\rm vco}}{2},\qquad \frac{T_{\rm vco}}{2}=\frac{1}{2\cdot6.72\,\text{GHz}}\approx 74\ \text{ps}\ \ (\text{slide quotes }83\ \text{ps @ 6 GHz})$$

This injects a SEL_CKFB-correlated error that corrupts the \(K_{\rm DTC}\) loop and re-introduces a spur slide p35.

(2) Signal path

Observable
Same PHE / \(e[k]=\operatorname{sign}(\text{PHE})\). slide p36
Actuator
vco_dcc added into the DTC code, sign set by SEL_CKFB: +1 → push CKDTC back by \(\Delta t_{\rm err}/2\); −1 → pull-in. slide p36
Target
\(\texttt{vco\_dcc}\to \Delta t_{\rm err}/2\). derived
Error signal
\(e[k]\) (shared 1-bit comparator). slide p40
Regressor
\(\texttt{SEL\_CKFB}[k]\in\{+1,-1\}\), the deterministic ±1 phase-select. slide p36
Update law
\(\texttt{vco\_dcc}[k{+}1]=\texttt{vco\_dcc}[k]+\mu_1\,\texttt{SEL\_CKFB}[k]\,(e[k]-e[k{-}1])\). derived

(3) Mathematical model

derived (derivations.md §7.2):

$$\text{PHE}[k]=\texttt{SEL\_CKFB}[k]\,\big(\Delta t_{\rm err}-2\,\texttt{vco\_dcc}[k]\big)+\text{noise}$$ $$\texttt{vco\_dcc}[k{+}1]=\texttt{vco\_dcc}[k]+\mu_1\,\texttt{SEL\_CKFB}[k]\,e[k]\;\;\longrightarrow\;\;\frac{\Delta t_{\rm err}}{2}$$

Intuition: correlating \(e[k]\) with the known ±1 SEL_CKFB sequence acts as a synchronous detector — it isolates the alternating duty-error component (which is perfectly correlated with SEL_CKFB) from random comparator noise (which is not). The slide-40 form applies a \((1-z^{-1})\) high-pass on \(e[k]\) before correlating slide p40.

(4) Convergence analysis

  • Why it is robust. The regressor is a pure deterministic square wave; any uncorrelated noise averages out, so the loop locks even with a noisy comparator (model uses comp_noise=0.05).
  • Stability / step size. Same first-order LMS bound; \(\mu_1=0.02\) assumption [A17] is chosen for ≈1-µs settling — much faster than the gain loop because the error is a strong, structured ±1 signal.
  • Limit cycle. 1-bit error → small dither around \(\Delta t_{\rm err}/2\); settling measured on a 200-sample moving average.
  • Interaction. Without vco_dcc, the SEL_CKFB-correlated error masquerades as gain error and the \(K_{\rm DTC}\) loop chases its tail — running both loops makes the residual orthogonal.

(5) Numerical example

Model defaults: 20-ps VCO duty error slide p40 assumption [A18]. Note the model's internal \(\Delta t_{\rm err}\) maps to a target of 10 ps (= \(\Delta t_{\rm err}/2\)), per calibrations.json.

Function: simulate_vco_dcc() in models/calibration_models.py.

sel = 1.0 if (k % 2 == 0) else -1.0          # SEL_CKFB toggles each cycle
phe = sel * (dt_err - 2.0 * vco_dcc)         # uncorrected duty error
phe += rng.normal(0.0, comp_noise * dt_err)  # comparator noise
e = np.sign(phe) or 1.0
vco_dcc += mu * sel * e * (dt_err)           # sign-LMS, converges to dt_err/2

C. CKREF duty-cycle calibration (ckref_dcc) — sign-LMS

(1) Purpose / what error it fixes

The reference is doubled (CKREF ×2 → CKREFX2) before the DTC slide p39. If the original reference clock duty cycle ≠ 50%, the doubled clock has alternating long/short periods — every other reference cycle is timed wrong, producing an alternating-cycle phase error slide p40. This is the same kind of structured error as the VCO duty case, but on the reference side.

(2) Signal path

Observable
Shared \(e[k]=\operatorname{sign}(\text{PHE})\). slide p40
Actuator
ckref_dcc injected into the FCW path before the modified DSM: \(\text{FCW}'=\text{FCW}+\texttt{ckref\_dcc}\cdot\texttt{even\_cycle}\). slide p40
Target
\(\texttt{ckref\_dcc}\to\Delta t_{\rm ref}/2\). derived
Error signal
\((1-z^{-1})e[k]\) (high-passed 1-bit error). slide p40
Regressor
\(\texttt{even\_cycle}[k]\in\{+1,-1\}\), toggling every reference cycle. slide p40
Update law
\(\texttt{ckref\_dcc}[k{+}1]=\texttt{ckref\_dcc}[k]+\mu_3\,\texttt{even\_cycle}[k]\,(e[k]-e[k{-}1])\). derived

(3) Mathematical model

derived (derivations.md §7.3); structurally identical to the VCO case but regressed on even_cycle:

$$\text{PHE}[k]=\texttt{even\_cycle}[k]\,\big(\Delta t_{\rm ref}-2\,\texttt{ckref\_dcc}[k]\big)+\text{noise}$$ $$\Delta t_{\rm ref}=\frac{(\text{duty}\%-50)}{100}\,T_{\rm ref},\qquad \texttt{ckref\_dcc}[k{+}1]=\texttt{ckref\_dcc}[k]+\mu_3\,\texttt{even\_cycle}[k]\,e[k]$$

(4) Convergence analysis

  • Synchronous detection again. even_cycle is a deterministic ±1 sequence; correlating with it picks out only the alternating-cycle error and rejects noise — same robustness argument as cal B.
  • Step size / settling. \(\mu_3=0.02\) assumption [A17] → ≈1-µs settling, same family of dynamics as vco_dcc.
  • Limit cycle & background. Same 1-bit dither; runs continuously to track reference-clock duty drift over temperature slide p40.

(5) Numerical example

Model uses 57% CKREF duty slide p40 assumption [A18] (slide injects "1 ns (57%)" CKREF duty error). With \(T_{\rm ref}=1/104\,\text{MHz}=9.6\) ns, the 7% asymmetry gives a target \(\Delta t_{\rm ref}/2\), per calibrations.json:

Function: simulate_ckref_dcc() in models/calibration_models.py.

dt_ref = (ckref_duty_pct - 50.0) / 100.0 * T_ref   # 7% of 9.6 ns ≈ 0.67 ns

ckref_dcc += mu * ec * e * dt_ref                  # sign-LMS, converges to dt_ref/2

D. Comparator/GM offset calibration (Vref_adj) — DC servo

(1) Purpose / what error it fixes — the key coupling

Every loop above uses \(e[k]=\operatorname{sign}(\text{PHE})\). Sign-LMS implicitly assumes \(e[k]\) is zero-mean when the system is correct. A static comparator + GM input offset adds a DC term to PHE, so \(\operatorname{sign}(\text{PHE})\) is biased toward +1 or −1 even at the correct operating point slide p27. That mean bias \(m\) feeds the bias term \(\mu_2\,m\,E\{\Phi_e\}\) into the gain loop → \(K_{\rm DTC}\) converges to the wrong value. This calibration is the prerequisite for all the others.

(2) Signal path

Observable
The mean of \(e[k]\) (leaky-integrated). slide p28
Actuator
A 1-bit ΔΣ-dithered ΔV-DAC sets Vref_adj on Vref1. slide p28
Target
Vref_adj → cancels the input-referred offset (32 mV) so \(\overline{e[k]}\to0\). slide p40
Error signal
\(e[k]\) directly (its sign drives the servo). slide p28
Regressor
None — it is a DC servo on the mean (regressor = constant 1). derived
Update law
\(\texttt{Vref\_adj}[k{+}1]=\texttt{Vref\_adj}[k]+\mu_{\rm os}\,e[k]\). derived

(3) Mathematical model

derived (derivations.md §7.4) — a sign-LMS on the mean, i.e. a DC servo. The offset referred to phase uses the SPD gain \(K_{\rm SPD}\):

$$V_{\rm os,rad}=\frac{V_{\rm os}}{K_{\rm SPD}},\qquad K_{\rm SPD}=\frac{K_{\rm slope}}{2\pi f_{\rm ref}}=\frac{6\,\text{GV/s}}{2\pi\cdot104\,\text{MHz}}\approx 9.19\ \text{V/rad}$$

slide p20 derived. Update drives the effective offset \(V_{\rm os,rad}-\texttt{Vref\_adj}/K_{\rm SPD}\) to zero:

$$e[k]=\operatorname{sign}\!\big(\text{PHE}+V_{\rm os,rad}-\tfrac{\texttt{Vref\_adj}}{K_{\rm SPD}}\big),\qquad \texttt{Vref\_adj}[k{+}1]=\texttt{Vref\_adj}[k]+\mu_{\rm os}\,e[k]$$

(4) Convergence analysis

  • Coarse DAC → residual. The ΔV-DAC is a coarse 1-bit element slide p28, so the servo settles to within a few-% residual (model uses a tol of 0.08, not 0.01). It is slower than B/C because the only "signal" is the small DC mean of a noisy 1-bit stream.
  • Offset → bias coupling (the emphasis). If you skip this servo, the gain loop's converged \(\hat K\) is biased. The model quantifies it: an injected offset (80 LSB) drives the gain loop to +22.6% error derived (calibrations.json: offset_bias_pct = 22.6). That is why cal_dtc_gain.png shows the "+uncal offset (BIAS!)" trace landing far above \(K_{\rm true}\).
  • Background. Runs continuously; tracks offset drift over temperature.
Order matters. Because the offset servo is the slowest of the four and gates the correctness of the others, in practice the offset loop must be settled (or co-adapting with a larger time constant) before the gain estimate can be trusted. The ≈22% bias is the cost of getting this wrong. derived

E. (Survey) Polynomial DTC nonlinearity correction (\(g_1,g_2,g_3\))

This is a survey technique (not the proposed Wu calibration set); we model the concept for completeness. slide p31

(1) Purpose

The DTC's intrinsic static INL is dominated by a 2nd-order term plus a smaller 3rd-order term slide p17 assumption [A8]. A wrong code-to-delay curvature → fractional spurs. NLC pre-distorts the code with a cubic polynomial so the total delay is linear in \(D_{\rm AQ}\) (the accumulated DSM phase).

(2)–(3) Signal path & model

Three parallel LMS loops, regressors \([D, D^2, D^3]\) derived (derivations.md §7.5):

$$D_{\rm DCW}=g_1 D + g_2 D^2 + g_3 D^3,\qquad e = (g_1^{\rm true}-g_1)D + (a_2-g_2)D^2 + (a_3-g_3)D^3$$ $$g_i[k{+}1]=g_i[k]+\mu_i\,e[k]\,D^{\,i},\qquad g\to(g_1^{\rm true},a_2,a_3)$$

Here \(g_1\) absorbs the linear gain (= \(K_{\rm DTC}\)) and \(g_2,g_3\) cancel the intrinsic INL coefficients \(a_2,a_3\). slide p31 slide p9

(4)–(5) Convergence & numbers

Three LMS loops with steps \((\mu_1,\mu_2,\mu_3)=(0.03,0.02,0.01)\); model defaults \(a_2=0.18, a_3=0.04\), 10% initial gain error. The peak INL collapses from 0.220 → ≈0 (1.07×10⁻⁴) after convergence, per calibrations.json.

(6)–(7) Python model & walkthrough

regress  = np.array([D, D**2, D**3])
residual = (g1_true-g[0])*D + (a2-g[1])*D**2 + (a3-g[2])*D**3

The one coupling to remember: the offset servo (D) is the keystone. An uncalibrated 32-mV offset biases the \(K_{\rm DTC}\) gain estimate (A) by ≈22.6% — large enough to undo the DTC's quantization-noise cancellation and bring back a fractional spur. The other three loops also rely on a zero-mean \(e[k]\). Calibrate the offset, and the rest fall into place. derived slide p27

Where these calibrations sit in the budget

Once all loops are converged, the residual DSM quantization noise is essentially cancelled (slide-42 budget shows ΣΔM QE ≈ 0.3%, "cancelled") and the integrated jitter is dominated by VCO (51.8%) and REF+DTC (≈37.8%), totaling 87.6 fs model ≈ 87.5 fs slide p42 / −51.6 dBc model ≈ −51.7 dBc slide p42 at \(f_{\rm out}=6.72\) GHz. The calibrations are what let the small-DR, high-gain DTC-assisted PD deliver that number — see the noise-budget page for the full breakdown.
