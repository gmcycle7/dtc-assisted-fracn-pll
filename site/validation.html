4. Frequency ↔ time cross-check

The two models are written independently — one is a closed-form s-domain (LTI) integration of PSDs, the other steps the loop in discrete time and Welch-estimates the PSD of the resulting phase-error sequence. If both arrive at the same jitter, a whole class of conceptual and arithmetic errors is ruled out. Over the same integration band they land at 88.2 fs (time) vs 87.4 fs (freq), a difference of only +0.08 dB derived.

Why the agreement isn't perfect (and shouldn't be)

  • Different Nyquist bands. The time-domain sim is sampled at the reference rate, so its Nyquist edge is $f_\text{ref}/2 \approx 52$ MHz, while the analytic integration runs to a wider $\sim$100 MHz span. The far-out tail therefore carries slightly different energy in the two models derived.
  • Finite record length. A finite-length sequence gives a PSD estimate with variance; the Welch estimate fluctuates around the true PSD, contributing a fraction of a dB textbook (Welch 1967).
  • Windowing. The window used for the Welch PSD has a power-normalization factor $U$ and finite spectral leakage; we correct for $U$ exactly (derivations.md §9) but leakage at band edges remains textbook.
  • Discrete-time vs continuous-time. The s-domain NTFs are continuous; the time model is a discrete-time (z-domain) loop. They match for $f \ll f_\text{ref}/2$ but diverge as $f$ approaches Nyquist, where the bilinear/ZOH mapping warps the response derived.

+0.08 dB is comfortably inside the <1.5 dB tolerance the brief sets for this check derived.

5. Type-I vs type-II loop

Open-question #1 asks which loop the silicon actually used: slide 39 shows both a proportional ($V_\text{ctrl,P}$) and an integral ($V_\text{ctrl,I}$) path into the VCO — the signature of a type-II loop — while slide 20 describes the SPD path as a first-order IIR, which reads more type-I slide p39, p20. Rather than guess, we model a type-II loop with a programmable proportional path and expose a type-I switch, then compare them head-to-head.

Type-II (two poles at origin)

  • Zero static phase error — the integrator drives steady-state error to 0.
  • Harder low-frequency reference suppression — the extra integrator keeps loop gain high at low offset, so it tracks (rather than rejects) slow reference content.
  • Can peak near $f_c$ if phase margin is low (gain peaking > 0 dB).

Type-I (one pole at origin)

  • First-order roll-off, unconditionally well-behaved.
  • No peaking in the closed-loop response.
  • Non-zero static phase error against a frequency ramp (the price of dropping the second integrator).

The trade is the textbook one textbook (Gardner): type-II buys zero static error at the cost of possible peaking and less low-frequency rejection; type-I is simpler and peaking-free but leaves a static error. Both are implemented so the page is correct whichever the chip used — this is how we resolve open-question #1 without inventing a fact.

Assumption. The budget and KPI numbers on this site use the type-II configuration with a programmable proportional path assumption [A1/A2 loop]. If you can confirm the measured chip's topology, the type-I switch is already in models/frequency_domain_model.py. needs citation (which topology the measured chip used).

8. Known limitations & open questions

These are the points where the slides are genuinely ambiguous and we chose not to guess silently. Each is taken from docs/open_questions.md; where we made a working assumption it is stated so you can correct it. None of these change the headline 87.6-fs result by more than a few percent, but they bound how literally each per-source number should be read.

1. Type-I vs type-II loop (open-q #1). Slide 39 shows proportional + integral paths (type-II) while slide 20's SPD reads type-I-ish slide p39, p20. We model type-II and expose a type-I switch (see §5). Needs confirmation of the measured chip's topology. needs citation
2. The ±3 dB DTC QN convention (open-q #2). The printed $\Phi_\text{DTC,QN}=(2\pi T_\text{res})^2/12\cdot f_\text{ref}$ reproduces −163 dBc/Hz only with the $/12$ reading; the single-sided textbook result uses $/6$ — a factor-2 (3 dB) choice slide p15. We adopt the slide's convention and flag it. needs citation (exact convention vs [Wu'19]).
3. Absolute noise floors are back-solved (open-q #7). Slide 42 gives only percentages of the 87.5-fs budget, not absolute dBc/Hz per source (except DTC QN/thermal) slide p42. Our per-source PSD levels assumption [A12–A15] are back-solved to reproduce those percentages — plausible but not unique. With the per-source PN curves we could lock them exactly.
4. DSM-QN magnitude is illustrative (open-q, §2 + #5). Slide 42 reports the ΣΔM quantization error as ≈0 % after DTC cancellation slide p42; our residual 4.5 fs (~0.3 %) shows the cancellation working but its exact magnitude depends on the modified-DSM internals, which are only sketched slide p34.
5. Modified-DSM internals (open-q #5). The "×2 / ÷2 / Acc / $z^{-1}$" network that produces $N_\text{DIV,tmp}$, $\text{SEL\_CKFB}$ and the half-range $\Phi_e(n)$ is only sketched on slide 34 slide p34. 9. Assumptions to challenge

Section 8 lists what we chose not to guess. This section is the active version: the five most load-bearing assumptions, reframed as experiments you should run on the model. A good engineer doesn't trust a number because it matched a slide — they push on the number until it either breaks or earns its place. Each box below has the same three parts: state it (what we assumed), consequence if wrong (what moves if the assumption is false), and how to test (the concrete experiment to run in the code). None of these are settled; they are the seams where a curious student can do real work.

Why these five and not the other four (#5 modified-DSM internals, #6 doubler rate, #3 cal LSB, #4 regressor toggling)? Those change local behavior — a cal waveform, a converged value — but not the headline 87.6-fs budget or the loop design. The five below each touch a load-bearing result: the per-source split, the cancelled residual, the loop order, the reported dBc number itself, and the chosen bandwidth. Break one of these and a KPI moves.

Challenge 1 — The noise floors are back-solved, not measured [A12–A15]

State it. Slide 42 gives only percentages of the 87.5-fs total (VCO 51 %, REF 39 %, MMD 6 %, SPD+GM 4 %) slide p42, not absolute dBc/Hz per source. So we back-solved each floor to reproduce those percentages assumption [A12–A15]: VCO −116.5 dBc/Hz @ 1 MHz, REF −168 dBc/Hz white + −143.5 @ 10 kHz flicker, MMD −165 dBc/Hz, SPD+GM −167 dBc/Hz derived from [A12–A15]. A back-solved set is plausible but not unique — many floor combinations integrate to the same percentages.

Consequence if wrong. The 87.5-fs total is anchored to the slide and won't move much, but the per-source attribution is only as good as the floors. If the real VCO is quieter than our fitted −116.5 dBc/Hz, its 51 % share shrinks and the REF/DTC share must grow to keep the total — which changes which block you'd redesign first. The whole point of a budget is to tell you where to spend effort; a mis-attributed budget sends you optimizing the wrong block.

How to test. The slides themselves give you a check: slide 50 (out of our scope) measures the VCO at −120 dBc/Hz @ 1 MHz single-core / −122.5 dual-core @ 6 GHz slide p50 — that is ~3.5–6 dB quieter than our fitted −116.5. Re-solve the budget with the real curve:

# In models/frequency_domain_model.py, PLLParams:
#   change vco_pn_1MHz from -116.5 to the slide-50 value, scaled to 6.72 GHz.
#   6.72 vs 6.00 GHz adds 20*log10(6.72/6.00) = +0.98 dB to L(f):
p.vco_pn_1MHz = -120.0 + 0.98   # single-core slide-50, freq-scaled  -> -119.0
# re-run and read the new budget split:
python models/run_all.py        # regenerates freq_domain.json budget[]

Then ask: does the VCO still carry 51 %? With ~2.5 dB less VCO noise its variance drops by ~100.25 ≈ 1.8×, so its share falls toward the high-30s % and the REF/DTC bucket rises — meaning the design becomes reference-limited, and the lever to pull is the DTC/REF path, not the oscillator. If the percentages no longer match slide 42 after the swap, that is the information: the slide's VCO must have been noisier than slide 50's, or the band/convention differs. Either way you've learned something the fitted model was hiding.

Challenge 2 — The DSM-noise magnitude is illustrative; only the cancelled residual is load-bearing

State it. Our model carries a raw ΣΔM quantization noise and then shows the DTC cancelling it down to a residual 4.5 fs (~0.3 %) derived, while slide 42 reports the post-cancellation DSM share as ≈0 % slide p42. The raw DSM magnitude we inject is illustrative — it depends on the modified-DSM internals that slide 34 only sketches slide p34 assumption [A5] m=2 MASH1-1. The number that actually matters to the budget is not the raw DSM noise but the residual that survives cancellation, because that is what adds to the total.

Consequence if wrong. The raw DSM level is a red herring — making it 6 dB bigger or smaller barely moves the 87.6-fs total as long as cancellation tracks it. What is load-bearing is the cancellation accuracy. The residual scales as the squared DTC-gain error: from the CAL analysis, a gain error ε leaves an in-band floor $S=\varepsilon^2(2\pi)^2 S_{Q,\text{dsm}}|H_\text{ref}|^2$, i.e. $\varepsilon=10^{-4}\to4.5$ fs, $10^{-3}\to45$ fs, $10^{-2}\to449$ fs derived (CAL). So a 1 % residual gain error alone (449 fs) would dominate the entire 87.6-fs budget. The "≈0 %" on slide 42 is a statement about the calibration, not about the DSM.

How to test. Don't tune the raw DSM — sweep the residual gain error ε and watch the budget, confirming the model reproduces 4.5 / 45 / 449 fs at ε = 1e-4 / 1e-3 / 1e-2:

import numpy as np
# residual DSM contribution vs DTC-gain cancellation error eps
for eps in (1e-4, 1e-3, 1e-2):
    # sigma_t scales linearly with eps; 1e-4 -> 4.5 fs (verified anchor)
    sigma_fs = 4.5e-15 * (eps / 1e-4) * 1e15
    print(f"eps={eps:.0e}  residual ~ {sigma_fs:6.1f} fs")
# -> need eps < ~2e-4 to keep the residual under 10 fs

Then separately, in the time-domain model, scale the raw DSM injection up by 6 dB with ε held fixed: the total should barely budge, proving the raw magnitude is illustrative. The takeaway: when someone says "DSM noise is 0 % after cancellation," the engineering content is in the ε < 2e-4 requirement on the calibration, not in the DSM number itself.

Challenge 3 — Type-I vs type-II is assumed, and it changes the loop's character

State it. We model a type-II loop (two poles at the origin: proportional + integral path into the VCO, the slide-39 signature) and expose a type-I switch slide p39, p20 assumption [A1/A2 loop]. The budget and all KPIs use type-II. But slide 20's SPD reads as a first-order IIR, which is more type-I-like — the deck is genuinely ambiguous (open-q #1), and the measured chip's topology is needs citation.

Consequence if wrong. This is not a cosmetic switch. Type-II gives zero static phase error but can peak near $f_c$ (we model 1.70 dB gain-peaking, ~25 % phase-step overshoot, from a closed-loop zero at $w_z=2\pi\cdot402$ kHz) derived (LOOP) and suppresses low-frequency reference content less aggressively (the second integrator keeps loop gain high at low offset). Type-I is first-order, peaking-free, but leaves a static error against a frequency ramp textbook (Gardner). The peaking directly raises the in-band reference/DTC contribution near $f_c$; if the real chip were type-I, our ~1.7-dB peaking is fictitious and the REF+DTC share is slightly overstated.

How to test. Flip the switch and re-integrate — the comparison is already wired (§5):

# models/frequency_domain_model.py exposes a loop-type switch:
p.loop_type = "type1"   # vs the default "type2"
python models/run_all.py
# compare three things between the two runs:
#   1) gain-peaking (type-II ~1.70 dB  ->  type-I ~0 dB)
#   2) the REF+DTC budget share (peaking pulls it up under type-II)
#   3) closed-loop poles: type-II has the wz=2pi*402 kHz zero that drives overshoot

Decision rule: if you can confirm the silicon's topology from the paper, pick it and delete the switch. If you can't, the honest move is exactly what §5 does — carry both and report which KPIs are topology-sensitive (peaking, overshoot, low-offset reference rejection) versus which are not (the far-out VCO tail, the total to first order).

Challenge 4 — The ±3 dB SSB/DSB IPN convention is a choice, and it's a factor of two

State it. We report single-sided phase noise with $\mathcal{L}(f)=10\log_{10}(\tfrac12 S_\phi)$ assumption [A19], but slide 42's IPN is double-sided slide p42. Relatedly, the printed DTC quantization-noise formula $\Phi_\text{DTC,QN}=(2\pi T_\text{res})^2/12\cdot f_\text{ref}$ reproduces −163 dBc/Hz only with the /12 reading; the textbook single-sided result uses /6 slide p15 (open-q #2). Each of these is a factor-of-two — exactly 3 dB — hiding in a convention, not in physics.

Consequence if wrong. A 3-dB convention error doesn't change the silicon, but it changes every dBc number you quote, and worse, it can move silently. If one source PSD is entered SSB and another DSB, their quadrature sum is wrong by an amount between 0 and 3 dB depending on the mix — and an IPN of −51.6 dBc reported under the wrong convention is off by 3 dB, which in jitter is a factor of $10^{3/20}=1.41$× (87.6 fs would read as 62 or 124 fs). Comparisons across papers (FoM tables, slide 4) become apples-to-oranges. This is the single most common way published PLL numbers fail to reproduce.

How to test. Force the model to be self-consistent and verify both endpoints:

# 1) The Parseval check is the convention's truth-teller:
#    integral of S_phi(f) over the band MUST equal var(phi).
#    time_domain.json: parseval_var_time 1.498e-5 vs parseval_var_psd 1.428e-5  (<0.5 dB) -> OK
# 2) Toggle the SSB/DSB flag and confirm the IPN moves by exactly 3.01 dB:
import numpy as np
print(10*np.log10(2))           # 3.01 dB  -- the whole ambiguity, in one line
# 3) Check the DTC-QN anchor:  /12 -> -163 dBc/Hz (slide),  /6 -> -160 dBc/Hz
#    if your number lands on -160, you've silently switched to the single-sided /6 form.

The discipline: pick one convention, write it at the top of the model, and let the Parseval identity ($\int S_\phi\,df=\operatorname{var}\phi$) police it. If Parseval holds, your factor-of-two is consistent end-to-end even if it disagrees with a given slide — and then a known 3-dB offset to the slide is a label, not a bug.

Challenge 5 — f_c = 1.5 MHz is NOT the jitter optimum (the optimizer finds ~2.1 MHz)

State it. We use a loop bandwidth $f_c=1.5$ MHz assumption [A1] (the slide says "BW trades REF vs VCO" but gives no number; 1.5 MHz ≈ $f_\text{ref}/70$ is the standard choice). But when we let the model optimize bandwidth for minimum jitter, it finds $f_c^*\approx2.1$ MHz giving ~85.6 fs — so the design's 1.5 MHz / 87.6 fs is slightly conservative, ~2 fs above the achievable floor derived (BW sweep).

Consequence if wrong. Quantitatively small (87.6 vs 85.6 fs is 0.2 dB). But conceptually it matters: the model's headline number is not the minimum the loop can hit, so anyone reading "87.6 fs" as "the best this loop does" is mistaken. More importantly, the gap tells you the design trades raw jitter for something the single-number optimizer doesn't see.

Now the engineering reasons a sane designer picks 1.5 over the 2.1-MHz optimum, each checkable:

  • The optimum is flat. 0.2 dB of jitter is below measurement repeatability — you are buying nothing real by chasing it. derived
  • Higher $f_c$ = more peaking and more reference/spur feed-through. A wider loop passes more of the reference-path content (REF+DTC, fractional spurs) to the output; with the type-II zero already giving 1.70 dB peaking (the peak sits near 0.70 MHz; crossover is at 1.5 MHz), widening to 2.1 MHz worsens both peaking and the −56…−70 dBc DTC/INL spurs that sit inside the loop band derived (SPURS). The jitter optimizer integrates these away; a spec sheet with a spur mask does not.
  • Sampled-loop stability margin. At 1.5 MHz, $f_c/f_\text{ref}\approx1/70$, so the ZOH half-sample lag ($\approx-\pi f/f_\text{ref}$) erodes phase margin only slightly. Push $f_c$ up and that erosion grows linearly — you start spending the 60° PM margin derived (LOOP) [A2].
  • Robustness to PVT. $K_\text{VCO}$, $K_\text{SPD}$ and the loop-filter values drift with process/temperature; sitting below the optimum leaves headroom so that when the loop widens under PVT it moves toward the optimum rather than past it into peaking/instability.

So the test isn't just "find the minimum-jitter $f_c$" — it's "plot jitter, peaking, worst spur, and PM-erosion on the same $f_c$ axis and find the bandwidth that satisfies all the masks." That is almost always a bit below the jitter-only optimum, which is exactly why 1.5 MHz is a defensible choice even though 2.1 MHz wins the single number. Lock time scales as ~$1/f_c$ derived, so 1.5 MHz also costs only ~40 % more settling (~1.13 µs) — a price most receivers gladly pay for margin.

The meta-lesson. Every one of these five is a place where a single quoted number ($f_c$, an IPN in dBc, a 51 % VCO share) compresses an engineering decision into a scalar. The job of validation isn't to defend the scalar — it's to re-expand it into the decision and check the decision still holds. Run the five experiments above and you'll understand this PLL better than the budget table alone could ever teach you.
