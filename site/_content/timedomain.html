Time-Domain Noise Analysis

A discrete-time, sample-by-sample simulation of the DTC-assisted fractional-N PLL that injects each physical noise source at its real node, lets the loop shape it, then extracts phase noise and jitter — and reproduces the frequency-domain budget (88.2 fs vs 87.4 fs) as an independent cross-check.

Why build a time-domain model at all? The frequency-domain model (Page 6) multiplies each source PSD by an analytic noise transfer function (NTF). That is fast but it assumes the NTFs and the small-angle linearization. The time-domain model makes no such assumption: it runs the actual difference equations of the loop, sample by sample, with noise sequences that have the right statistics. If the two models agree (they do, to derived +0.08 dB), each validates the other. This page is "Section H.4 — cross-check" of the brief, plus the source-model and sanity-check sections.

A. The noise-source models

Every noise mechanism in the PLL is a random process with a characteristic spectral shape. In the time domain we cannot inject a PSD directly — we must synthesize a sample sequence whose PSD matches the target. The recipe is always the same three steps: (1) pick the spectral type, (2) generate samples and scale them so the realized PSD hits the target level, (3) inject as a phase at the correct loop node, where the loop turns it into output phase and ultimately jitter. The conversions are standard derived in derivations.md §8–9.

The model is sampled once per reference cycle, so the simulated Nyquist band is $f_{ref}/2 = 52$ MHz. A white phase source with single-sided PSD $S_\varphi$ [rad²/Hz] must therefore carry total variance spread over that band derived:

$$\sigma_\varphi^2 \;=\; S_\varphi \cdot \frac{f_{ref}}{2}, \qquad S_\varphi = 2\cdot 10^{\,\mathcal{L}/10}$$

so a target of $\mathcal{L}=-160$ dBc/Hz on a 104-MHz grid becomes a Gaussian sequence with $\sigma_\varphi=\sqrt{2\cdot10^{-16}\cdot 52\times10^6}\approx 1.02\times10^{-4}$ rad per sample. (This exact scaling is checked by test_noise_rms_scaling to within 1.5 dB — see §F.) DSP — Welch / Parseval

Reference (CKREF)

Type: white floor + flicker (1/f). PSD units: rad²/Hz.

Generated: gen_white_phase (Gaussian, flat) plus gen_flicker_phase (white shaped by $f^{-a/2}$ in the FFT domain, −10 dB/dec slope). Scaled: flicker normalized so $S_\varphi(10\,\text{kHz})$ hits the anchor. Levels: floor $-168$ dBc/Hz, flicker $-143.5$ dBc/Hz @ 10 kHz assumption[A12]. Node: reference input → low-pass NTF $\times N$.

VCO

Type: white-FM (−20 dB/dec, random-walk phase) + 1/f³ flicker. PSD units: rad²/Hz.

Generated: gen_vco_phase integrates white frequency steps (cumsum) → 1/f² phase, adds a flicker bump below the corner. Scaled: normalized to $S_\varphi(1\,\text{MHz})$. Levels: $-116.5$ dBc/Hz @ 1 MHz, corner 150 kHz assumption[A13]. Node: VCO accumulator → high-pass NTF (loop suppresses it at low offset).

DTC (QN + thermal)

Type: two white sources. PSD units: rad²/Hz.

Generated: two gen_white_phase draws summed. Levels: quantization $-163$ dBc/Hz slide p15, thermal $-171$ dBc/Hz slide p16. The QN floor follows $\Phi_{DTC,QN}=\frac{(2\pi T_{res})^2}{12}f_{ref}$ with $T_{res}=400$ fs derived (§3). Node: reference path → same low-pass NTF as CKREF.

SPD + GM

Type: white, input-referred. PSD units: rad²/Hz.

Generated: one gen_white_phase. Level: $-167$ dBc/Hz slide p20/assumption[A15]. The large $K_{SPD}=K_{slope}/(2\pi f_{ref})=9.18$ V/rad slide p20 is precisely why this floor is low — comparator/GM noise divides by the steep sampling slope. Node: at the phase detector output.

MMD (divider)

Type: white, input-referred. PSD units: rad²/Hz.

Generated: one gen_white_phase. Level: $-165$ dBc/Hz assumption[A12]/slide p42. Node: added at the $\div N$ feedback output, so it shares the reference-like low-pass NTF.

DSM quantization noise

Type: high-pass-shaped (MASH-$m$) at the divider. PSD units: cycles²/Hz: $S=\frac{1}{12 f_{ref}}\big[2\sin(\pi f/f_{ref})\big]^{2m}$ DSM — Riley JSSC'93. Node: feedback. But: the DTC cancels the in-band DSM QN (slide 9), so the model does not inject it — its residual is ≈0% in the budget. slide p42

Phase → jitter, the through-line. Whatever node a source enters, its contribution to output phase $\varphi_{out}$ is what we ultimately integrate. The chain is identical for all: a timing error $\Delta t$ at the reference becomes a reference phase $\varphi=2\pi f_{ref}\Delta t$; the loop shapes it; the output phase variance integrates to $\sigma_\varphi$; and jitter is $\sigma_t=\sigma_\varphi/(2\pi f_{out})$. Note the conversion uses $f_{out}=6.72$ GHz at the output, not $f_{ref}$. derived §8

B. Discrete-time loop simulation

The sampling phase detector fires once per reference period, so the entire loop is a discrete-time system clocked at $f_{ref}=104$ MHz (Nyquist $=52$ MHz). The continuous type-II loop (BW $f_c=1.5$ MHz assumption[A1], PM $=60°$ assumption[A2]) is mapped to two discrete integrators (the VCO phase accumulator and the PI integral) plus one zero (the PI proportional term). The realized gains are $K_p=5.07$, $K_i=0.265$, zero at $f_z=866$ kHz derived (from time_domain.json).

State-update equations

Each reference tick $k$ runs five lines; together they are the loop. The core is the VCO accumulator:

$$\varphi_{out}[k] \;=\; \underbrace{\varphi_{out}[k-1]}_{\text{prior phase}} \;+\; \underbrace{K_v\,\text{ctrl}}_{\text{frequency this step}} \;+\; \underbrace{\text{vco\_step}[k]}_{\text{VCO noise increment}}$$

Reading the four terms: (1) phase is a running accumulator — that is what an oscillator is, an integrator of frequency; (2) the control voltage ctrl sets the instantaneous frequency, scaled by $K_v$ (absorbed to 1 here); (3) vco_step[k] is the per-sample VCO phase increment (the loop sees the difference $\Delta\varphi_{vco}$ between consecutive samples, hence np.diff of the VCO phase); and implicitly (4) the result feeds back through $\div N$ to close the loop next sample. derived

phi_out[k] = phi_out[k-1] + Kv*ctrl + vco_step[k]
VCO phase accumulator: integrate frequency (= $K_v\cdot$ctrl) and add VCO noise. The discrete integrator $\frac{z^{-1}}{1-z^{-1}}$ that gives the loop its second pole.
phi_fb = phi_out[k] / N + mmd_noise[k]
Feedback divider $\div N$ ($N=64.62 = 6720/104$) derived [A3], with MMD/divider noise injected at its output.
phi_ref = ref_noise[k] + dtc_noise[k]
Reference path: CKREF phase noise + DTC (QN+thermal). The DTC cancels the DSM quantization error, so it is not re-added here.
e = phi_ref - phi_fb + spd_noise[k]
Sampling phase detector: error $=\varphi_{ref}-\varphi_{fb}$, plus SPD/GM input-referred noise. This is $K_{PD}$ in the linear model, here normalized.
acc += e
PI integral accumulator — the loop's other integrator, giving type-II behavior (zero steady-state phase error).
ctrl = Kp * e + Ki * acc
PI loop filter: proportional ($K_p$, sets the zero / phase margin) + integral ($K_i$). Output drives the VCO next sample.

C. Extracting PSD, phase noise, and jitter

From the single phase sequence $\varphi_{out}[k]$ we recover everything via a Welch periodogram. The single-sided PSD is normalized so its integral equals the variance (Parseval) DSP — Welch 1967:

$$S_\varphi(f) \;=\; \frac{2}{f_s\,M\,U}\,\big|\text{FFT}(w\cdot\varphi)\big|^2, \qquad U=\overline{w^2}\ \text{(window power)}, \qquad \int S_\varphi(f)\,df \approx \mathrm{var}(\varphi)$$

Then the standard derived §8 conversions give phase noise, integrated phase, and jitter:

$$\mathcal{L}(f) = 10\log_{10}\!\Big(\tfrac{1}{2}S_\varphi(f)\Big)\ \text{[dBc/Hz]}, \qquad \sigma_\varphi=\sqrt{\int_{f_{lo}}^{f_{hi}} S_\varphi(f)\,df}, \qquad \sigma_t=\frac{\sigma_\varphi}{2\pi f_{out}}$$

The $\tfrac12$ factor is the single-sided (SSB) Leeson / IEEE-1139 convention assumption[A19]; the ±3 dB SSB↔DSB ambiguity is acknowledged throughout. Integration runs over [1 kHz, $0.95\cdot f_{ref}/2$] = [1 kHz, 49.4 MHz] assumption[A16].

freq, Sxx = U.welch_psd(res["phi_out"], fs, nseg=8)   # single-sided, integral = var
L = U.Sphi_to_L(Sxx)                                   # L(f) = 10log10(0.5*Sxx)
sigma_phi = U.integrate_phase_noise(freq, Sxx, f_lo, f_hi)   # sqrt of band integral
jitter_fs = U.rms_jitter_from_sigma_phi(sigma_phi, f_out) * 1e15   # /(2*pi*f_out)

The two RMS jitter numbers agree to +0.08 dB ($20\log_{10}(88.2/87.4)$) — far inside the 1.5 dB test tolerance. derived The Parseval check matches: the variance computed directly from the time series, $\mathrm{var}(\varphi)=1.50\times10^{-5}$ rad², equals the integral of the PSD over the full resolved band, $1.43\times10^{-5}$ rad² (within ~5%, expected from Hann windowing and the dropped DC bin). DSP — Parseval

Residual differences — why not exactly zero?
  • Nyquist band limit. The time-domain model only resolves up to $f_{ref}/2=52$ MHz; the headline frequency-domain budget integrates to 100 MHz assumption[A16]. The 52–100 MHz VCO tail is excluded here, which is exactly why the same-band freq number (87.4 fs) is quoted for comparison rather than the full 87.6 fs model ≈ 87.5 fs slide p42.
  • Finite record length. $2^{20}$ samples averaged over 8 Welch segments leaves spectral estimation variance — the gray trace is noisy, especially at low offset where few bins exist.
  • Windowing. The Hann window's power normalization $U=\overline{w^2}$ and 50% overlap introduce a small (sub-dB) bias and account for most of the var(time) vs var(PSD) gap.
  • Discrete vs continuous. The z-domain NTF $\big[\,$using $z=e^{j2\pi f T_s}\big]$ deviates from the s-domain NTF as $f\to f_{ref}/2$ (the $\sin$ vs linear warp), a fraction of a dB near Nyquist.
That two completely different computational paths land within 0.08 dB is strong evidence both models — and the slide-42 budget they reproduce — are correct.

Self-check

Q1. You want to inject a white phase source at $\mathcal{L}=-160$ dBc/Hz into the model, which is sampled once per reference cycle. What per-sample Gaussian standard deviation $\sigma_\varphi$ do you generate, and why does the answer depend on $f_{ref}$?
Show answer
$\sigma_\varphi=\sqrt{S_\varphi\cdot f_{ref}/2}=\sqrt{2\cdot10^{-16}\cdot 52\times10^6}\approx 1.02\times10^{-4}$ rad per sample, using $S_\varphi=2\cdot10^{\mathcal{L}/10}$ (double-sided→single-sided) and the simulated Nyquist band $f_{ref}/2 = 52$ MHz (since $f_{ref}=104$ MHz). A white sequence spreads its total variance flat across the resolved band, so the per-sample variance scales with the bandwidth $f_{ref}/2$; a higher sample rate means the same PSD level carries more total variance per sample. derived §8–9 / DSP — Parseval
Q2. After running the loop, the variance of the output phase computed directly from the time series is $1.50\times10^{-5}$ rad², but the integral of the Welch PSD over the resolved band is $1.43\times10^{-5}$ rad². Is this a bug, and what guarantees these should match?
Show answer
Not a bug. Parseval's theorem guarantees $\int S_\varphi(f)\,df \approx \mathrm{var}(\varphi)$ when the PSD is normalized by the window power $U=\overline{w^2}$: $S_\varphi(f)=\frac{2}{f_s M U}|\text{FFT}(w\cdot\varphi)|^2$. The two numbers agree within ~5%, and the small gap is expected from Hann-window power normalization and the dropped DC bin — not a normalization error. The automated test_welch_psd_normalization gates this to < 10% for a white input. DSP — Welch 1967 / Parseval
Q3. The time-domain run reports 88.2 fs RMS jitter, but the full frequency-domain budget is 87.6 fs to 100 MHz. Why is 87.4 fs — not 87.6 fs — the correct number to compare against, and what is the resulting cross-check agreement?
Show answer
The time-domain model only resolves up to its Nyquist limit $f_{ref}/2 = 52$ MHz, so the 52–100 MHz VCO tail is physically absent from the simulation. To compare fairly you must integrate the frequency-domain model over the identical band [1 kHz, 49.4 MHz], which gives 87.4 fs (vs. the full-band 87.6 fs model ≈ 87.5 fs slide p42). The cross-check is then $20\log_{10}(88.2/87.4)\approx +0.08$ dB — well inside the 1.5 dB test tolerance, confirming the two independent computational paths agree. derived §H.4 / assumption[A16]
