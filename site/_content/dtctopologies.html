DTC Topologies & Survey Techniques

A digital-to-time converter (DTC) turns a digital code into a precise edge delay. The Wu deck surveys a whole zoo of DTC circuit variants and digital-assist linearization tricks from many groups before settling on its own analog-sampling architecture. This page collects those survey techniques — one card per idea, each with its paper tag and the slide it comes from. These are not the proposed Wu core (that lives in Architecture and Calibrations); they are the menu of options the talk compares against.

How to read this page. Every technique below is tagged with its originating paper, e.g. slide p21 [Ru'15]. Cards marked as survey are documented conceptually; the project's models/code focus on the proposed Wu architecture and its four background calibrations. The two structural range-reduction techniques (½-range with two VCO phases, 1/8-range with eight ring-oscillator phases) are the bridge to the calibration story.

Why DTC range is the master tradeoff

Before looking at circuits, fix the one parameter that governs everything: the DTC dynamic range (DR), i.e. the total delay the DTC must span. In a fractional-N PLL the divider edge wanders relative to the reference by up to ±(DSM order)·TVCO/2, and the DTC has to delay the reference by exactly that much to make the phase detector see ≈0 error (integer-N-like). The bigger that wander, the bigger the DR — and DR drives noise, power, and linearity all at once.

Slide 33 collects the scaling laws for an n-bit RC variable-slope DTC with unit step $t_{res}$:

$$\mathrm{DR} = 2^{n}\,t_{res} = 2^{n}\,\ln 2\cdot R\,C_{LSB}$$

Quantization noise slide p33

QN $\propto t_{res}^{2}$. Finer step → lower in-band quantization floor. slide p15

Thermal noise slide p33

$$\mathcal{L}\cong 10\log_{10}\!\Big[2kT\,f_{ref}\,\tfrac{(2\pi)^2}{\ln 2}\,\mathrm{DR}\cdot R\Big]$$

so $\mathcal{L}\propto \mathrm{DR}$ (noise rises with range).

Power slide p33

power $\propto \mathrm{DR}^{2}$. The buffer that drives the ramp must charge a bigger cap array faster — cost grows quadratically.

Linearity slide p33

Smaller DR → better linearity (less INL across the swept code, smaller fractional spurs and noise folding).

The punchline (slide 33): shrinking DR improves all of QN, thermal noise, power, and linearity simultaneously. So the most leveraged thing you can do is not build a fancier DTC — it is to reduce the range the DTC must cover. That motivates the two structural tricks below.

Proposed range reduction: ½-range with two VCO phases slide p34

Idea. Instead of letting the DTC cover the full residual quantization-error range, use a modified MMD + DSM to select between two VCO phases — CKFB1 and CKFB2, where CKFB2 = CKFB1 delayed by ½TVCO — via a control bit SEL_CKFB. Switching CKFB1→CKFB2 adds a fixed +½TVCO of feedback delay, so the modified DSM only has to leave a half-range residual for the DTC to mop up: $\Phi_e\in[0,\,0.5\,T_{VCO}]$ instead of $[-T_{VCO}/2,\,+T_{VCO}/2]$. slide p34

Pro. Halving DR (per the slide-33 laws) directly improves QN, thermal noise, power, and linearity of the DTC — the cheapest "free lunch" in the design. derived from slide 33

Con. It relies on the two VCO phases being exactly ½TVCO apart. A non-50% VCO duty cycle breaks this ($\Delta t_{err}=\Delta t - T_{VCO}/2\neq 0$), injects a SEL_CKFB-correlated error, and re-introduces a spur — which is exactly why the VCO duty-cycle calibration exists. slide p35

Proposed technique [Wu'21] — see the full story (½-range, VCO duty error, vco_dcc sign-LMS) in Calibrations.

Link to calibrations. Range reduction and the digital calibrations are two halves of the same plan: shrink the DR structurally, then null the residual analog imperfections (DTC gain K_DTC, VCO duty cycle, CKREF duty cycle, comparator/GM offset) with background sign-LMS loops. All four are on the Calibrations page.

Survey: DTC circuit variants

RC variable-slope DTC — the baseline slide p14

Idea. The reference edge buffers onto an RC network: a resistor $R$ charges an n-bit programmable capacitor array $C_{LSB}$, producing a voltage ramp $V_{dly}$ whose slope depends on the selected code. A comparator fires when the ramp crosses threshold, so the digital code sets the edge delay. The unit delay is slide p15:

$$T_{res} = \ln 2 \cdot R \cdot C_{LSB}$$

The input-referred quantization-noise PSD at CKREF is $\Phi_{DTC,QN}=\frac{(2\pi T_{res})^2}{12}\,f_{ref}$; with $T_{res}=400$ fs and $f_{ref}=104$ MHz this gives −163 dBc/Hz, and a 10-bit array yields DR = 400 ps. slide p15

Pro. Simple, compact, low power; the slope is set by an explicit, easily-programmed cap array. It is the reference against which every other variant is judged.

Con. Because each code changes the slope, the comparator crossing time is code- and slope-dependent → static INL (parabolic, <2 LSB with a fixed cap; ±0.2 LSB DNL with DEM) slide p17 plus dynamic/memory effects from supply settling (±0.6 LSB measured) slide p18.

Constant-slope (I/C) DTC slide p21 [Ru'15]

Idea. Keep the ramp slope fixed and instead vary the start point / threshold. A current DAC (IDAC) and resistor DAC (RDAC) charge a load capacitor $C_L$ with a constant slope, so the delay is $\Delta t_{DTC}=\int dV\cdot C_L(V)/(K\cdot I)$. Holding the slope constant removes the slope-dependent comparator nonlinearity of the variable-slope baseline. slide p21

Pro. Constant slope → more uniform comparator behavior across codes, improving large-signal linearity vs. the RC baseline.

Con. Needs high supply (≈1.5 V) for headroom, and residual nonlinearity still comes from the I/C product — $C_L(V)$ and the IDAC are themselves voltage-dependent. slide p21

Survey technique — [Ru'15] (constant-slope I/C DTC).

Inverse-constant-slope (ICS) DTC slide p22 [Dartizio'23]

Idea. A coarse-fine structure (DFF + mux clocked at $f_{VCO}$) where the charging current itself is made proportional to the voltage-dependent capacitance, so the delay becomes $\Delta t_{DTC}=\int C_L(V)\,dV/(K\cdot I_G(V))$. Because $I_G(V)$ tracks $C_L(V)$, the result is immune to both $I_G(V)$ and $C(V)$ nonlinearity. slide p22

Pro. First-order cancellation of the dominant voltage-dependent error sources → excellent intrinsic linearity without polynomial pre-distortion.

Con. Requires a precise fine DTC and a current generator that accurately mirrors the cap nonlinearity — added analog complexity. slide p22

Survey technique — [Dartizio'23] (inverse-constant-slope DTC).

Pseudo-differential DTC slide p23 [Xu'24]

Idea. Split the conversion across two half-range DTCs operating differentially. Each half has an even-symmetric (parabolic-like) INL; subtracting the two paths makes the even-symmetric INL cancel, leaving only the smaller odd residual. slide p23

Pro. Cancels the dominant even-order static nonlinearity by construction, and each half only spans half the range (range-reduction benefit too).

Con. Needs two matched DTC paths; mismatch between the halves limits the achievable cancellation.

Survey technique — [Xu'24] (pseudo-differential DTC).

Survey: digital-assist linearization & range-reduction

DTC NLC — polynomial calibration slide p31 [Park'21]

Idea. Leave the DTC analog-imperfect and fix it in the digital domain with a polynomial pre-distortion of the DTC control word. The accumulated DSM phase $D_{AQ}$ is mapped through a third-order polynomial whose coefficients are learned by LMS: slide p31

$$D_{DCW} = g_1\,D_{AQ} + g_2\,D_{AQ}^{2} + g_3\,D_{AQ}^{3}$$

where $g_1$ is effectively K_DTC and $g_2,g_3$ capture the curvature. slide p31

Pro. Cancels static INL down to a max of ≈50 fs after NLC, purely with background digital adaptation — no analog redesign. slide p31

Con. Only corrects the static, polynomially-modeled part; dynamic/memory effects (slide 18) are not captured by a memoryless polynomial. Needs three adaptive coefficients to converge.

Survey technique — [Park'21] (polynomial DTC NLC). The model concept is reproduced in code; see simulate_polynomial_nlc (models/calibration_models.py).

Reverse-concavity variable-slope DTC slide p32 [Rossoni'24]

Idea. The variable-slope DTC's INL is parabolic with a fixed concavity. Here a voltage DAC adjusts the upper resistor $R_U$ to bend the transfer curve the other way — introducing a controllable reverse-concavity that cancels the native curvature. An adaptation loop searches for the DAC code that flattens the residual INL. slide p32

Pro. Linearizes the cheap RC variable-slope DTC in the analog domain with a single tuning knob ($R_U$ DAC) instead of a polynomial.

Con. Cancels one dominant curvature term; the analog tuning must track PVT, and the adaptation must find/hold the right DAC code.

Survey technique — [Rossoni'24] (reverse-concavity variable-slope DTC).

POC-DTC: zero-mean phase-error tracking slide p29 [Renukaswamy'23]

Idea. A phase-offset-calibrated (POC) DTC built around a PFD-CP plus a coarse-fine DTC and a 1-bit TDC. The reference/feedback time-of-flight offset $\Delta T_{of}$ drifts with PVT; the POC loop tracks it so that the sign-of-error Error_Sign[k] stays zero-mean — which is exactly the condition a sign-LMS calibration needs to converge without bias. slide p29

Pro. Keeps the phase-detector error zero-mean across PVT, preventing the offset-induced convergence bias that plagues sign-LMS loops (the same problem the proposed Wu offset/ΔV-DAC cal solves differently). slide p27

Con. Needs a coarse+fine DTC and a 1-bit TDC dedicated to tracking $\Delta T_{of}$ — extra hardware beyond the main DTC.

Survey technique — [Renukaswamy'23] (POC-DTC zero-mean PHE).

1/8 DTC range reduction with 8 RO phases slide p38 [Hwang'22]

Idea. Push the range-reduction idea further than the ½-range two-phase scheme: tap eight ring-oscillator (RO) phases and use an 8:1 mux plus a window generator and quadruple-timing-margin phase selector (QTM-PS) so the DTC only spans 1/8 of the original range. A first-order ΔΣM handles the residual, and an RO phase-error calibrator (RPEC / DRPEC LMS) corrects the inter-phase mismatch. slide p38

Pro. 8× DR reduction → large QN/thermal/power/linearity wins per the slide-33 laws; the QTM phase selector gives generous timing margin. derived from slide 33

Con. Requires eight matched RO phases (SOUT[7:0]) and a dedicated background LMS calibrator for the RO phase-error mismatch — more phases to generate and match than the proposed two-phase scheme.

Survey technique — [Hwang'22] (1/8 DTC-range reduction with QTM phase selector). Contrast with the proposed ½-range two-VCO-phase approach (slide 34) used in this project.

Why constant-/inverse-constant-slope linearize — the integrals

The three slope-based DTCs (variable, constant, inverse-constant) all turn a code into a delay by charging a node until a comparator trips. What separates them is where the code enters the trip-time integral. Doing the integral once for each makes the linearity argument concrete: the variable-slope DTC puts the code in the denominator (so a threshold error becomes a code-dependent, parabolic INL), the constant-slope DTC moves the code out of the slope (so the threshold error becomes a harmless constant delay), and the inverse-constant-slope DTC arranges the integrand so the two voltage-dependent factors divide out entirely.

The universal trip-time integral

A comparator fires when the ramp $V(t)$ crosses a threshold $V_{th}$. The delay from the start of the ramp to the trip is, in general,

$$\Delta t \;=\; \int_{V_{start}}^{V_{th}} \frac{dV}{\,\dot V(V)\,}, \qquad \dot V \equiv \frac{dV}{dt}.$$

Everything below is a special case of this one integral. The INL story is entirely about how $\dot V$ and $V_{th}$ depend on the digital code. derived/model

Variable-slope (RC baseline): code in the slope → parabolic INL

In the RC variable-slope DTC slide p14 the code programs the cap array, hence the slope at threshold. Linearize the ramp near the crossing with a local slope $\left.\dot V\right|_{th}$ that is set by the code. The trip time measured from a fixed nominal crossing is

$$t_{trip} \;=\; -\,\frac{\Delta V_{th}}{\;\left.\dfrac{dV}{dt}\right|_{th}\;},$$

where $\Delta V_{th}$ is any fixed threshold/offset error (comparator offset, finite-gain crossing error). The key observation: the slope in the denominator changes with code, so the same fixed $\Delta V_{th}$ produces a different time error at every code. A constant numerator over a code-varying denominator is exactly a reciprocal/curved map — to leading order it bends the transfer curve into the parabolic INL the deck reports for this DTC (<2 LSB fixed-cap, plus ±0.6 LSB dynamic). slide p17 slide p18

Show the algebra

Let the ideal crossing be at time $t_0$ and voltage $V_{th}$, with code-dependent slope $m(\text{code})=\left.\dot V\right|_{th}$. A threshold perturbation $\Delta V_{th}$ shifts the crossing time by

$$t_{trip}=-\frac{\Delta V_{th}}{m(\text{code})}.$$

For an RC ramp $V(t)=V_{DD}\big(1-e^{-t/RC}\big)$ the mid-rail slope is $m=\tfrac{V_{DD}}{2RC}\ln 2$; the code sets $C=C_{LSB}\cdot \text{code}$, so $m\propto 1/\text{code}$ and

$$t_{trip}\;\propto\;-\,\Delta V_{th}\cdot \text{code}.$$

Meanwhile the intended delay is $\Delta t = \ln 2\cdot R\,C_{LSB}\cdot\text{code}=T_{res}\cdot\text{code}$ slide p15. The error term $t_{trip}$ grows with code in the same direction the delay does, and once you carry the next order of the exponential (the crossing is not exactly at mid-rail across codes) the residual is quadratic in code — a parabolic INL. derived/model

Constant-slope (I/C): code out of the slope → threshold error becomes a constant delay

The constant-slope DTC slide p21 [Ru'15] charges $C_L$ with a fixed current $I$, so the slope is

$$\dot V = \frac{I}{C_L} = \text{const (independent of code)},$$

and the code instead moves the start point / threshold. Put a fixed threshold error $\Delta V_{th}$ into the universal integral with a constant slope:

$$\delta t \;=\; \frac{\Delta V_{th}}{\dot V} \;=\; \frac{\Delta V_{th}\,C_L}{I} \;=\; \text{const, independent of code}.$$

Now the same imperfection that bent the variable-slope curve is just a code-independent delay — a constant offset that shifts every code equally and therefore cancels out of the INL (INL is deviation from a straight line; a constant adds to the intercept, not the curvature). The deck writes the conversion as $\Delta t_{DTC}=\int dV\,C_L(V)/(K\,I)$. slide p21 The residual nonlinearity that remains is only what is inside that integral: the voltage dependence of the $C_L(V)$/$I$ product, which is why this DTC still needs ≈1.5 V headroom to keep $I$ and $C_L(V)$ well-behaved. slide p21

One-line contrast. Variable-slope: $\delta t=\Delta V_{th}/\dot V(\text{code})$ — error varies with code (parabolic INL). Constant-slope: $\delta t=\Delta V_{th}\,C_L/I$ — error is the same at every code (a removable constant). Moving the code out of the slope is what converts a curvature into an offset. derived/model

Inverse-constant-slope (ICS): make the integrand self-cancel

Constant-slope still leaves $C_L(V)$ nonlinearity in the integrand. ICS slide p22 [Dartizio'23] goes one step further: it makes the charging current itself proportional to the instantaneous capacitance, $I_G(V)\propto C_L(V)$. Substitute into the universal integral:

$$\Delta t_{DTC}=\int \frac{C_L(V)\,dV}{K\,I_G(V)} \;=\; \int \frac{C_L(V)\,dV}{K\cdot \big[\kappa\,C_L(V)\big]} \;=\; \frac{1}{K\kappa}\int dV \;=\; \frac{\Delta V}{K\kappa}.$$

The voltage-dependent factor $C_L(V)$ appears in both numerator and denominator and divides out, so the delay depends only on the integration limits $\Delta V$ and a constant — immune to both $I_G(V)$ and $C(V)$ to first order, with no polynomial pre-distortion. slide p22 That self-cancellation is the whole point of "inverse-constant-slope": the slope is deliberately made the inverse of the cap so the product is flat.

Reading the three integrals together. All three share $\Delta t=\int dV/\dot V$. The design choice is purely where you let the code and the nonidealities live: in the slope (variable → parabolic INL), out of the slope (constant → INL becomes a removable offset), or matched into both halves of the integrand (ICS → the nonideality cancels itself). The proposed Wu design keeps the cheap variable-slope DTC and instead attacks the INL with range reduction + background calibration, the topic of the next two sections.

Range-reduction ledger (dB & ×power)

Section "Why DTC range is the master tradeoff" above gave the four slide-33 scaling laws. Here is the bookkeeping: take the full-range DTC as the 0 dB / 1× reference, then read off what ½-range (two VCO phases, slide 34) and 1/8-range (eight RO phases, slide 38) buy you. The single lever is the dynamic range DR; everything else follows from $\mathrm{DR}=2^{n}t_{res}$, $\mathrm{QN}\propto t_{res}^{2}$, $\mathcal{L}\propto \mathrm{DR}$, $P\propto \mathrm{DR}^{2}$. slide p33

Convention for the ledger. Hold the bit count $n$ fixed and shrink the range, so the unit step scales with the range: $t_{res}\propto \mathrm{DR}$. Then quantization noise $\mathrm{QN}\propto t_{res}^{2}\propto \mathrm{DR}^{2}$, thermal noise $\mathcal{L}\propto \mathrm{DR}$, and power $P\propto \mathrm{DR}^{2}$. (Equivalently you could keep $t_{res}$ fixed and drop bits — same DR, same DTC-noise contribution to the loop, but you lose codes instead of step size.) Show the dB arithmetic

With $t_{res}\propto\mathrm{DR}$ at fixed bit count:

  • QN. $\mathrm{QN}\propto t_{res}^2$. Halving DR: $10\log_{10}(\tfrac12)^2=20\log_{10}\tfrac12=-6.02$ dB. Eighth: $20\log_{10}\tfrac18=-18.06$ dB.
  • Thermal ℒ. $\mathcal{L}\propto\mathrm{DR}$. Halving: $10\log_{10}\tfrac12=-3.01$ dB. Eighth: $10\log_{10}\tfrac18=-9.03$ dB.
  • Power. $P\propto\mathrm{DR}^2$. Halving: $(\tfrac12)^2=\tfrac14$. Eighth: $(\tfrac18)^2=\tfrac1{64}$.

Note the two noise mechanisms scale differently with the same range cut: QN (a quantization variance) goes as DR² but thermal ℒ (an integrated-noise/range product, slide 16/33) goes only as DR. So range reduction is a stronger lever on the quantization floor than on the thermal floor — useful when the DTC QN, not its thermal noise, is the limiter. derived from slide 33

What the ledger does not charge for. The dB wins above are the DTC's own contribution. They ignore the new error each scheme introduces: ½-range needs two VCO phases exactly $T_{VCO}/2$ apart (a non-50% duty cycle injects a SEL_CKFB-correlated spur → the VCO duty-cycle calibration, slide 35-37), and 1/8-range needs eight matched RO phases (inter-phase mismatch → a dedicated RO phase-error LMS, slide 38). The structural win is real but it is paid for with an extra background calibration. slide p35 slide p38

Why the highlighted row wins (2 sentences). The RC variable-slope core is the smallest, lowest-power, and lowest-QN starting point (−163 dBc/Hz at $T_{res}$=400 fs), and its one weakness — parabolic INL — is precisely the kind of low-order, slowly-drifting error that a background sign-LMS calibration plus the cheap ½-range structural trick can remove without any extra analog DAC, TDC, or matched-phase burden. Every more-linear circuit on the list pays for that linearity in headroom (I/C), analog complexity (ICS), matching (pseudo-diff, 8-phase RO), or PVT-tracking analog tuning (reverse-concavity), whereas the proposed combination shifts the cost into low-area digital adaptation — exactly the trade a scaled FinFET process favors. derived/model
