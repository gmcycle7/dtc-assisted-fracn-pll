Units cheat-sheet

The single most common source of confusion in PLL math is mixing up phase (radians or cycles), time (seconds), and frequency (hertz). They are all linked by the same two conversions, so keep these on a sticky note:

  • Phase ↔ time. A phase error of $\varphi$ rad on a clock of frequency $f$ corresponds to a time error $\Delta t = \dfrac{\varphi}{2\pi f}$. Going the other way, $\varphi = 2\pi f\,\Delta t$. textbook
  • Phase ↔ cycles. $1\ \text{cycle} = 2\pi\ \text{rad}$. The DSM emits quantization noise in cycles2/Hz; multiply by $(2\pi)^2$ to get rad2/Hz. derived
  • PSD ↔ dBc/Hz. Single-sided phase PSD $S_\varphi(f)$ is in rad2/Hz; the log-scale phase noise is $\mathcal{L}(f)=10\log_{10}\!\big(\tfrac12 S_\varphi\big)$ dBc/Hz. The factor $\tfrac12$ is the single-sideband (SSB) convention used throughout. textbook [A19]
  • PSD ↔ jitter. Integrating the PSD gives a variance $\sigma_\varphi^2=\int S_\varphi\,df$ (rad2); divide the RMS phase by $2\pi f_\text{out}$ to get RMS jitter in seconds: $\sigma_t=\dfrac{\sigma_\varphi}{2\pi f_\text{out}}$. textbook
  • VCO gain. Datasheets quote $K_\text{VCO}$ in Hz/V; the phase-domain gain is $2\pi K_\text{VCO}[\text{Hz/V}]/s$ (rad/s/V over $s$). Always check which one a formula expects. derived textbook
  • $s$ and $\omega$. In all transfer functions $s=j\omega$ and $\omega=2\pi f$. Phases are in rad, times in s, frequencies in Hz. textbook

The same triangle, run backward (dBc → mrad → fs). The box above goes jitter → dBc. Reading a budget plot usually hands you the opposite: a single IPN number, from which you want the RMS phase and the jitter. Invert the SSB definition $\text{IPN}=10\log_{10}\!\big(\tfrac12\sigma_\varphi^2\big)$ to get $\sigma_\varphi=\sqrt{2\cdot 10^{\,\text{IPN}/10}}$, then divide by $2\pi f_\text{out}$.

Given. The model's headline $\text{IPN}=-51.6$ dBc (SSB) derived · $f_\text{out}=6.72$ GHz slide p42.

Step 1 — dBc → rad. $\sigma_\varphi=\sqrt{2\cdot 10^{-51.6/10}}=\sqrt{2\cdot 6.92\times10^{-6}}=3.72\times10^{-3}\ \text{rad}=3.72\ \text{mrad}.$

Step 2 — rad → s. $\sigma_t=\dfrac{\sigma_\varphi}{2\pi f_\text{out}}=\dfrac{3.72\times10^{-3}}{2\pi(6.72\times10^{9})}=8.81\times10^{-14}\ \text{s}=88.1\ \text{fs}.$

A tiny KaTeX legend

The recurring math notation, decoded once:

  • $s = j\omega$, $\omega = 2\pi f$ — Laplace variable on the imaginary axis; the model evaluates transfer functions at $s=j2\pi f$.
  • $z^{-1}$ — one-sample (one reference cycle) delay in the discrete-time cal loops; $(1-z^{-1})^m$ is the DSM's $m$th-order noise shaping.
  • $G \equiv G_\text{ol}(s)$ — shorthand for the open-loop gain inside $N\,G/(1+G)$ and $1/(1+G)$.
  • $\to$ — "tends to" (limit), e.g. $H_\text{ref}\to N$ at DC, $H_\text{vco}\to 1$ at high frequency.
  • $|\cdot|$, $\angle\cdot$ — magnitude and phase of a complex transfer function; PM uses $\angle G_\text{ol}(j\omega_c)$.
  • $\hat{g}$ — the adaptive estimate of a true parameter $g$ (e.g. $\hat{K}_\text{DTC}$ converging to $K_\text{DTC}$).
  • $\mathbb{E}\{\cdot\}$ — expectation (statistical average), used in LMS convergence arguments.
  • $kT$ — Boltzmann's constant times absolute temperature ($\approx4.14\times10^{-21}$ J at 300 K); the $kT/C$ in DTC thermal noise.
