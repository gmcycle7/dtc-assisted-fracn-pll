Architecture — The DTC-Assisted Sampling Fractional-N PLL

A frequency synthesizer where a digital-to-time converter (DTC) "pre-cancels" the divider's fractional jitter so a high-gain analog sampling phase detector sees almost zero phase error — like an integer-N loop — and four background calibrations keep that cancellation perfect over PVT.

The 10-second intuition

In any fractional-N PLL you divide the VCO by a number that is, on average, fractional (here $N \approx 64.62$ derived [A3] (= 6720/104)). But a digital divider can only divide by whole integers, so a delta-sigma modulator (DSM) dithers the integer divide ratio cycle-by-cycle to average to the fraction. That dithering makes the feedback edge (CKFB) jump around in time by up to several VCO periods. A classic charge-pump PLL therefore feeds its phase detector a large, noisy, nonlinear phase error — that error is the DSM quantization error (QE), and it is the headache of every frac-N design slide p6.

The DTC-assisted trick: the DSM knows the QE exactly at every cycle (it is the residue inside the accumulator). So instead of dumping that QE into the phase detector, we delay the reference clock by the same amount using a programmable delay element (the DTC). The reference edge now arrives at the detector at the same instant the feedback edge does. The phase detector sees $\approx 0$ — exactly as if we were running integer-N slide p9.

Why this is powerful

With the QE removed before the detector, the detector no longer needs a wide linear range. You can build a high-gain, small-dynamic-range phase detector — a sampling PD — which gives lower in-band noise, better linearity, and lower power than a PFD-charge-pump slide p9.

What it costs you

The DTC itself adds quantization and thermal noise into the reference path, and its gain (codes per picosecond) drifts with PVT. The whole back half of this study — the four calibrations — exists to make the DTC cancellation accurate and to keep its noise contribution small.

Reference path & the DTC trick

In a frac-N loop the feedback edge wanders by up to $\pm(\text{order})\cdot T_{vco}/2$ relative to the reference, because the DSM keeps swapping the integer divide value slide p6. The DTC's job is to delay CKREF by exactly that wander so the two edges line up. The defining relation is

$$K_{DTC} = \frac{T_{vco}}{\Delta t_{DTC}} \quad\text{(DTC codes per VCO period)}$$ slide p9

and the delay command is the accumulated QE divided by that gain:

$$\Delta t[n] = \frac{\Phi_{QE}[n]}{K_{DTC}}, \qquad \text{DTC code} = K_{DTC}\cdot \Phi_e[n].$$ slide p9

When $K_{DTC}$ is calibrated to its true value, the delay exactly cancels the divider wander and the PD sees near-zero phase error after lock — "just like the integer-N case" slide p9. If $K_{DTC}$ is off (wrong picoseconds-per-code), the cancellation is partial and a residual phase error proportional to $(K_{DTC,true}-\hat{K}_{DTC})\cdot\Phi_e$ leaks into the loop derived (derivations §7.1) — which is exactly what the DTC-gain calibration nulls.

If you removed the DTC: the sampling PD would have to absorb the full DSM QE swing ($\pm$ several $T_{vco}$). That is far outside the SPD's small linear range, so it would saturate, fold the QE into in-band noise, and produce strong fractional spurs. The DTC is what makes the high-gain sampling PD usable at all.

Phase detector — the sampling PD (SPD)

The SPD works like a slope-based time-to-voltage converter. A slope generator starts a voltage ramp on the reference (CKDTC) edge; the feedback edge (CKFB) samples that ramp. The sampled voltage is held (zero-order hold) until the next cycle, so in the loop the SPD path looks like a 1st-order IIR filter slide p20. Its gain is

$$K_{SPD} = \frac{K_{slope}}{2\pi f_{ref}}.$$

With $K_{slope}=6$ GV/s and $f_{ref}=104$ MHz slide p20:

$$K_{SPD} = \frac{6\times10^{9}\ \text{V/s}}{2\pi \cdot 104\times10^{6}\ \text{Hz}} = \frac{57.7}{2\pi} \approx 9.18\ \text{V/rad}$$ derived (derivations §6)

Reading: a phase error $\varphi$ (rad) at the reference is a time error $\varphi/(2\pi f_{ref})$, which the slope $K_{slope}$ turns into a sampled voltage $K_{SPD}\cdot\varphi$.

Loop filter, VCO, and divider

Loop filter — Type-II

Slide 39 shows two control paths into the VCO: a proportional $V_{ctrl,P}$ and an integral $V_{ctrl,I}$ slide p39. The integral path gives the loop two poles at the origin (one from the VCO, one from the integrator) → Type-II, which tracks frequency offsets with zero steady-state phase error textbook (Gardner).

If you removed the integral path: the loop would be Type-I, leaving a static phase error and worse reference-spur suppression.

VCO — dual-core

A dual-core LC VCO generates the 6.72-GHz output slide p39 slide p42. It also provides the two phases (½$T_{vco}$ apart) used by the range-reduction trick. The VCO is the single largest noise and power contributor (12 mW, ≈51.8% of jitter) slide p39 slide p42.

If you removed it: no output — but more usefully, a noisier VCO directly raises the dominant 51.8% bucket.

Divider — MMD + modified DSM

The MMD divides by integers; the modified DSM dithers NDIV to realize the fraction and emits SEL_CKFB and $\Phi_e(n)$ slide p34 slide p39. The "modified" part halves the QE range the DTC must cover by choosing between two VCO phases slide p34.

If you removed the DSM: you could only synthesize integer multiples of $f_{ref}$ — no fine frequency resolution.

Type-II vs Type-I — an open ambiguity [open_questions #1]. Slide 39 clearly draws both a proportional ($V_{ctrl,P}$) and an integral ($V_{ctrl,I}$) path, which says Type-II. But slide 20 describes the sampled SPD path as a 1st-order IIR, which by itself reads as a Type-I sampling loop. We model a Type-II loop (proportional + integral) and expose a Type-I switch in the model; the exact topology of the measured chip is not stated on slides 1–42. assumption [A1, A2] (loop BW ≈ 1.5 MHz, PM ≈ 60°, both inferred, not on the slides).

On the reference doubler (CKREFX2)

The very first block in the signal chain — CKREF → ×2 → CKREFX2 on slide 39 — looks like a throwaway frequency multiply, but it is a deliberate design lever: doubling the reference halves the time the DTC must span every update and improves the in-band reference-path SNR. The price is a new error term — an alternating-cycle duty imperfection — which is exactly what the third calibration (ckref_dcc) exists to remove.

Why a designer doubles the reference

On slide 39 the input clock passes through a $\times 2$ doubler before the DTC, producing CKREFX2 slide p39. Three things happen when the update rate goes from $f_{ref}$ to $2f_{ref}$, and the first two are why you would want to do it.

1 · Smaller per-update QE wander → smaller DTC range

The DTC must absorb the divider's per-update timing wander, whose span is set by the DSM order: $\pm(\text{order})\cdot T_{vco}/2$ in VCO periods slide p6. But the time the DTC has to resolve that wander against is one reference period $T_{ref}=1/f_{ref}$. Run the loop at $2f_{ref}$ and each update spans a shorter window: less accumulated phase slips between adjacent edges, so the staircase command $\Delta t[n]=\Phi_{QE}[n]/K_{DTC}$ has to traverse a smaller dynamic range per step derived/model. A smaller required DTC range is the single best thing that can happen to a DTC: range $\propto$ QN, range$^2 \propto$ power, and INL grows with range — the whole slide-33 tradeoff chain ($\text{DR}=2^n t_{res}$, $\text{QN}\propto t_{res}^2$, power $\propto \text{DR}^2$) improves at once slide p33.

2 · Better in-band reference-path SNR

Two effects push the in-band floor down. First, doubling $f_{ref}$ halves the divide ratio $N=f_{out}/f_{ref}$, and every reference-path source is multiplied to the output by $N$ (gain $20\log_{10}N$ in PN), so halving $N$ buys $\approx 6$ dB of in-band headroom on the REF/SPD/MMD bucket derived textbook. Second, you take twice as many phase samples per second; for the same target loop bandwidth the per-update noise is averaged over more samples, lowering the contribution of the discrete-time detector path derived. Since the REF$+$DTC bucket is $\sim$37.8% of the budget (slide quotes 39%) slide p42, shaving it is worth real femtoseconds.

3 · The catch — DTC QN scales with $f_{ref}$

The one term that moves the wrong way is the DTC's own quantization noise. From slide 15 the input-referred DTC-QN PSD is $$\Phi_{DTC,QN}=\frac{(2\pi\,T_{res})^2}{12}\,f_{ref},$$ so $\Phi_{DTC,QN}\propto f_{ref}$ slide p15. Doubling the effective reference rate raises this floor by $10\log_{10}2\approx 3$ dB derived for a fixed $T_{res}$. The reason the doubler still wins is that the finer required range (effect 1) lets you use fewer bits / a smaller $T_{res}$, and the $N$-reduction (effect 2) lowers everything else — so the net REF$+$DTC bucket drops even though this one sub-term rises.

Reading the $f_{ref}$ dependence both ways. Notice that $\Phi_{DTC,QN}\propto f_{ref}$ is a per-cycle quantization variance times rate: each comparison snaps the edge to the nearest $T_{res}$ grid (variance $T_{res}^2/12$ in time), and you make $f_{ref}$ such comparisons per second, so the white timing-noise PSD scales linearly with rate textbook (quantization). The factor $(2\pi)^2$ converts a time error to a phase error at the carrier; the resulting $-163$ dBc/Hz uses $T_{res}=400$ fs, $f_{ref}=104$ MHz with the $/12$ (double-sided) convention slide p15. If the cal/DTC actually runs at $2f_{ref}=208$ MHz, plug that in and the floor rises $\approx 3$ dB — which is why the doubler is only a net win when paired with range reduction.

The duty-cycle problem a doubler creates

A clock doubler is usually built by XOR-ing the reference with a delayed copy of itself (one output edge per input edge, i.e. doubled rate). Any error in that internal delay — or any deviation from a 50% duty cycle on CKREFX2 — makes the even and odd half-periods unequal. The doubled clock then has two interleaved edge spacings, $T_{ref}/2+\delta$ and $T_{ref}/2-\delta$, that alternate every cycle derived/model assumption [A-dbl]. From the loop's point of view that is a deterministic timing error that flips sign on alternating reference cycles — an alternating-cycle (period) error, exactly the impairment slide 40 injects as "1 ns (57%) CKREF duty error" slide p40.

[A-dbl] — how the doubler is built. Slides 1–42 show the $\times 2$ block but not its internals. The XOR-of-delayed-copy implementation (and therefore the "duty error $\to$ alternating-cycle error" mechanism) is the standard textbook doubler and is consistent with slide 40 calling the impairment a CKREF duty-cycle error and correcting it with an even/odd-cycle regressor. We treat the exact circuit as an assumption. assumption textbook

This is why the calibration that fixes it correlates the 1-bit error with an even_cycle($\pm 1$) regressor that toggles every reference cycle: the alternating-cycle error is, by construction, the component of $e[k]$ that is in lock-step with even_cycle. The ckref_dcc loop is a sign-sign LMS — $(1-z^{-1})e[k]$ correlated with even_cycle($\pm1$), integrated, then injected into the FCW path before the modified DSM ($\text{FCW}'=\text{FCW}+\text{ckref\_dcc}$) so the divider pre-compensates the period imbalance slide p40. It converges in $\approx 1\,\mu$s, pulling the injected 1 ns down to $\approx 0.337$ ns residual (see the calibration table above) slide p40 derived.

Contrast with vco_dcc. The reference doubler's even/odd imbalance is the reference-side twin of the VCO duty error: vco_dcc uses a SEL_CKFB($\pm1$) regressor (toggling with the phase-select pattern) and adds its correction into the DTC code; ckref_dcc uses an even_cycle($\pm1$) regressor (toggling per reference cycle) and adds its correction into the FCW. These two regressors are mutually orthogonal under expectation, so the two loops co-adapt without fighting derived slide p40.

Q5. A reference doubler with imperfect 50% duty creates a specific kind of timing error. What is it called, which calibration corrects it, what regressor does that calibration use, and where is the correction injected? Contrast the regressor and injection point with the VCO duty-cycle cal.
Show answer
An imperfect-duty doubler produces an alternating-cycle (period) error: the even and odd half-periods differ, so the timing error flips sign every reference cycle (slide 40 injects this as a 1 ns / 57% CKREF duty error). Contrast: vco_dcc uses a SEL_CKFB($\pm1$) regressor (toggles with the phase-select pattern) and adds its correction into the DTC code.
