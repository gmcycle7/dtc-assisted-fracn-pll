The problem this deck solves

Modern radios — WiFi-7 and 5G FR2 (mmWave) — need a local oscillator (LO) so clean that its integrated RMS jitter is below 100 fs slide p.3. The reason is the modulation: as you pack more bits per symbol (64-QAM → 256-QAM → 1024-QAM → 4096-QAM), the constellation points crowd together and even a tiny amount of LO phase noise rotates a symbol into its neighbor's decision region. Slide 3 lists the required double-sideband integrated phase noise (DSB-IPN) per scheme: −30 dBc for 64-QAM, −33 dBc for 256-QAM, −44 dBc for 1024-QAM, and −47 dBc for 4096-QAM slide p.3. Translated to jitter at carriers of 7–47 GHz, all of these land below 100 fs RMS.

A fractional-N PLL is what lets you hit an arbitrary frequency (e.g. 6.72 GHz from a 104 MHz reference, an irrational-looking divide ratio derived $N = f_\text{out}/f_\text{ref} = 6720/104 = 64.62$). But fractional-N is much harder to make low-jitter than integer-N, for three reasons spelled out on slide 6:

1. ΔΣ quantization noise

To get a fractional divide ratio, a delta-sigma modulator (DSM) dithers the integer divider so its average is fractional. That dithering is quantization noise injected right at the divider. A higher-order MASH spreads it out but makes the instantaneous phase error larger: slide p.6 MASH1 keeps $\Phi_e \in [-T_\text{vco}/2, +T_\text{vco}/2]$, MASH1-1 doubles it to $[-T_\text{vco}, +T_\text{vco}]$, and MASH1-1-1 doubles again to $[-2T_\text{vco}, +2T_\text{vco}]$.

2. Phase-detector dynamic range

Because of that DSM dithering, the feedback edge wanders over a wide range every reference cycle. The phase detector must stay linear over that whole range — a fundamental conflict, because a high-gain (low-noise) PD usually has a small linear range. slide p.6

3. Fractional spurs & noise folding

Any PD nonlinearity demodulates the periodic DSM sequence into discrete fractional spurs, and folds high-frequency quantization noise back into the band of interest, raising the in-band floor. slide p.6

So the central tension is: you want a high-gain, low-noise, perfectly linear phase detector, but the DSM forces it to swallow a large, fast-moving phase error. Conventional designs lose either noise or linearity.

One-line architecture

The proposed idea, in one sentence

Put a DTC in the reference path that adds a programmable delay tracking the accumulated DSM quantization error, so the feedback edge and the (now-delayed) reference edge arrive together and the phase detector sees ≈ 0 phase error after lock — exactly like an integer-N PLL slide p.9. That lets you use a high-gain, small-dynamic-range sampling phase detector (low noise, low power). A stack of background digital calibrations keeps the cancellation exact over process, voltage and temperature slide p.39slide p.40.

The key relation is the DTC gain — how many DTC delay codes correspond to one VCO period:

$$K_\text{DTC} = \frac{T_\text{vco}}{\Delta t_\text{DTC}} \qquad\text{(codes per VCO period)} \quad\text{\small(slide p.9)}$$

The DTC is told to add a delay $\Delta t[n] = K_\text{DTC}^{-1}\,\Phi_\text{QE}[n]$, a staircase that exactly mirrors the divider's accumulated phase wander $\Phi_\text{QE}[n]$ slide p.9. If $K_\text{DTC}$ is right, the residual phase error collapses to zero and the PD operates in a tiny window around its highest-gain point.

Once the PD only sees a near-zero error, the design uses a sampling phase detector (SPD) — a slope generator plus a sample/hold — whose gain is enormous compared to a PFD–charge-pump. Slide 20 gives slide p.20

$$K_\text{SPD} = \frac{K_\text{slope}}{2\pi f_\text{ref}}, \qquad K_\text{slope}=6\ \text{GV/s},\ f_\text{ref}=104\ \text{MHz}\ \Rightarrow\ K_\text{SPD}\approx 9.2\ \text{V/rad}.$$

That high gain divides down the noise of everything after it (the transconductor, loop filter), which is why the whole thing can reach a sub-100-fs floor. The full assembled signal chain and all four calibrations live on the Architecture page.

What the noise budget looks like after calibration

The validation target is slide 42: with the PD, DSM quantization noise and divider noise all suppressed, the integrated phase noise is dominated by the two sources common to every PLL — the reference and the VCO. Our frequency-domain model reproduces this almost exactly.

Note that the contributions add in RMS (root-sum-square of $\sigma_\phi$), not linearly:

$$\sigma_{\phi,\text{tot}} = \sqrt{\textstyle\sum_i \sigma_{\phi,i}^2} \quad\text{\small(textbook: uncorrelated noise sources add in power)} \quad \text{\tiny t-std}$$
Why the budget shapes the design. Once the loop has killed the PD/DSM/divider terms, the only knob left is loop bandwidth: a wider bandwidth tracks (suppresses) more VCO noise but lets in more reference noise, and vice-versa. The 1.5 MHz / 60° design is the chosen REF-vs-VCO trade-off — see the Frequency-domain page. slide p.42

Learning roadmap

The site is organized as a guided path. Read it top to bottom the first time; jump around once you know the layout.

  1. Slide map — a one-row-per-slide index of all 42 slides: what each covers, which are background vs. the proposed Wu architecture vs. survey techniques, and where each equation lives.
  2. Architecture — how the full DTC-assisted sampling PLL is wired together (CKREF → DTC → SPD → GM → VCO → MMD → modified DSM), why the DTC goes in the reference path, and the integer-N-like phase-error cancellation.
  3. Blocks — block-by-block deep dives: the RC variable-slope DTC and its quantization/thermal noise and nonlinearity, the sampling PD gain and IIR dynamics, the modified MMD + DSM, and the half-range / 8-phase range-reduction tricks.
  4. Calibrations — the four background sign-sign-LMS loops: DTC gain ($K_\text{DTC}$), VCO duty-cycle (vco_dcc), reference duty-cycle (ckref_dcc), and comparator/GM offset, plus why each is needed and how fast each converges.
  5. Frequency-domain — the linear type-II phase model, loop transfer functions and noise-transfer functions, the Bode plot / phase margin, and how the per-source phase-noise budget is built.
  6. Time-domain — a sample-by-sample behavioral simulation, the time-series of the loop, per-source jitter accumulation, and the Parseval cross-check against the frequency-domain result.
  7. Code walkthrough — a line-by-line tour of the Python models that produce every figure on this site, mapping each line back to a slide formula.
  8. Examples — worked numerical examples: computing $K_\text{DTC}$, the DTC noise floors, $K_\text{SPD}$, and the jitter budget by hand, with the model as a check.
  9. References — the source deck, the author's own papers (Wu JSSC'19 / ISSCC'21 / RFIC'23), the survey works cited per slide, and the full provenance-tag legend.

How to use / reproduce

Regenerate every figure

All plots on this site are produced from the behavioral models. Re-run them with:

python models/run_all.py

This regenerates the figures in assets/figures/ (the fd_*, td_* and cal_* PNGs referenced throughout) and refreshes the JSON data the tables read from.

Verify the numbers

The tests/ directory checks the model against the deck's anchor numbers — for example that the modeled total reproduces slide 42 (87.6 fs / −51.6 dBc) and that the time-domain and frequency-domain jitter agree (88.2 fs vs 87.4 fs, +0.08 dB, Parseval matches) derived.
