1. Jitter ŌåÆ EVM ŌåÆ QAM SNR

A modulator with random phase wander smears each constellation point. For small angles the smear is just the phase itself, so the RMS error-vector magnitude equals the RMS phase in radians.

Intuition

Picture a 1024-QAM constellation: 1024 dots packed into a square. A symbol at unit radius, rotated by a small random angle $\phi$, lands a distance $\approx \phi$ (rad) away from its ideal spot ŌĆö that displacement is the error vector. The fraction of symbols that cross into a neighbor's decision region is set by how big $\phi$ is relative to the dot spacing. Denser constellations (more bits/symbol) have tighter spacing, so they demand a smaller $\sigma_\phi$ ŌĆö equivalently a more negative IPN.

Math

For small phase error, the error vector on a unit-power symbol is $\approx \sin\phi \approx \phi$, so $$\boxed{\;\text{EVM}_\text{rms}\approx \sigma_\phi\ (\text{rad})\;}$$ and the phase-noise-limited SNR is the inverse of that error power: $$\text{SNR}_\text{phase}=-20\log_{10}(\sigma_\phi)=-\text{IPN}\quad(\text{dB}).$$ derived/model The last equality uses the deck's convention $\text{IPN}=20\log_{10}\sigma_\phi$ (the same one-sideband $\tfrac12$ factor that defines $\mathcal{L}(f)$) so that an IPN of $X$ dBc costs you exactly $|X|$ dB of constellation SNR. slide p42 convention

2. Reciprocal Mixing (the receiver's view)

EVM is the transmitter's complaint. The receiver has a different, often stricter, complaint that lives at far offsets where the EVM integral has long since died: a strong adjacent blocker mixes with the LO's skirt and dumps noise right on top of your wanted signal.

Intuition

The LO is not a clean tone; it has phase-noise skirts. When a big out-of-band blocker hits the mixer, it convolves with that skirt and produces a noise floor inside your channel ŌĆö as if the blocker had been "painted" with the LO's phase noise and slid down to baseband. This is reciprocal mixing. Crucially it is set by $\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)$ at the blocker offset, which can be tens of MHz away ŌĆö a region governed by the VCO's far-out (thermal) floor, not by the in-loop jitter that dominates EVM. So this spec is frequently independent of, and orthogonal to, the integrated-jitter EVM spec.

Math

The blocker's contribution to in-channel power is its power, lowered by the LO phase noise at the blocker offset, integrated over the channel bandwidth: $$\boxed{\;\frac{C}{I}=P_\text{wanted}-\big[P_\text{blocker}+\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)+10\log_{10}\!\text{BW}\big]\;}\quad(\text{dB})$$ textbook with $\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)$ in dBc/Hz and BW in Hz. The $10\log_{10}\text{BW}$ converts the per-Hz skirt into the total noise power folded into the channel.

3. Jitter Figure of Merit

How do you compare a 60 fs / 50 mW synthesizer against a 90 fs / 14 mW one? You can't with jitter alone ŌĆö you need a metric that prices in power. The jitter FoM does exactly that, and it encodes a hard thermal law: jitter┬▓ ├Ś power is a constant.

Intuition

Every fundamental noise source (kT/C sampling, VCO tank thermal noise) scales as $1/\text{power}$: spend 2├Ś the current and you cut the noise power in half, i.e. jitter by $\sqrt2$. So the product $\sigma_t^2 \cdot P$ is roughly invariant for a given technology and topology ŌĆö it is the "exchange rate" between jitter and watts. The FoM just takes its log in dB.

Math

$$\boxed{\;\text{FoM}_\text{jitter}=10\log_{10}\!\Big[\big(\sigma_t\big)^2\cdot\frac{P}{1\,\text{mW}}\Big]\;}\quad(\text{dB, more negative = better})$$ textbook (Gao/Staszewski FoM) For this design, $\sigma_t=87.6$ fs and $P=14.2$ mW (VCO 12 + DTC 1.2 + MMD 0.5 + rest 0.5) slide p39: $$\text{FoM}=10\log_{10}\!\big[(87.6\times10^{-15})^2\cdot 14.2\big]=-249.6\ \text{dB}.$$ derived/model

Numbers ŌĆö the iso-FoM diagonals

The dual-core VCO lever. Combine $N_\text{core}$ identical VCO cores: the correlated signal adds coherently while uncorrelated phase noise averages, dropping $\mathcal{L}$ by $10\log_{10}(N_\text{core})$ ŌĆö ŌłÆ3 dB for a dual core ŌĆö but at $N_\text{core}\times$ power. derived/model Plug into the FoM: jitter power drops 2├Ś, power rises 2├Ś, so $\sigma_t^2 P$ ŌĆö and hence the FoM ŌĆö is unchanged. 4. Jitter Taxonomy: Absolute vs Period vs Cycle-to-Cycle

"Jitter" is three different numbers depending on what you difference. They all come from the same phase-noise PSD ŌĆö period and cycle-to-cycle jitter are just high-pass-weighted versions of the absolute spectrum.

Intuition

  • Absolute (long-term) jitter ŌĆö how far each edge has wandered from an ideal grid. This integrates the full PSD including the low-frequency hump, so it is the largest. This is what EVM and IPN use.
  • Period jitter ŌĆö the variation of one cycle's length: difference adjacent edges, $\Delta t_n = t_{n+1}-t_n$. Differencing is a $(1-z^{-1})$ operation, which high-pass weights the PSD, killing the slow wander. Smaller number.
  • Cycle-to-cycle jitter ŌĆö the variation between consecutive periods, a second difference $(1-z^{-1})^2$. Even more aggressively high-pass; matters for things like SerDes timing recovery and digital setup/hold.

Math

Period jitter applies a first-difference weighting to the absolute phase PSD: $$S_\text{period}(f)=\big|1-e^{-j2\pi f T_0}\big|^2\,S_\phi(f),\qquad T_0=1/f_\text{out}.$$ The factor $|1-e^{-j2\pi f T_0}|^2 = 4\sin^2(\pi f T_0)$ rolls off as $f^2$ at low offset ŌĆö that is the high-pass weighting that suppresses the close-in hump. Cycle-to-cycle squares the weighting again. derived/model textbook (period/c2c jitter)

5. The Design Tradeoff: REF-vs-VCO Bandwidth U-curve

Every PLL designer fights the same battle. Open the loop bandwidth and you suppress more VCO noise but pass more reference/DTC noise; close it and you do the reverse. The total integrated jitter is a U-shaped function of bandwidth, with a single optimum.

Intuition

The loop is a low-pass for everything entering at the reference (REF phase noise, DTC quantization + thermal, the divider/DSM path) and a high-pass for VCO noise (Section 1's NTFs). Widening the bandwidth $f_c$:

  • lets the loop track and suppress more VCO noise (good ŌĆö VCO is 51% of the budget slide p42),
  • but admits more of the REF + DTC noise into the output band (bad ŌĆö REF+DTC is ~39% slide p42).

The crossover of these two trends is the U-curve minimum. Slide 42 explicitly notes the bandwidth "is chosen to trade off REF vs VCO ŌĆö common to all PLLs." slide p42
