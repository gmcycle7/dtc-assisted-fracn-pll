System Impact — Why Sub-100 fs?

A PLL designer can stare at dBc/Hz curves forever, but the only thing the system architect cares about is: does this clock let me close my link? This page walks the chain from integrated phase jitter to the four places it actually hurts — constellation EVM, receiver blocker rejection, the jitter–power figure of merit, and the subtle distinction between absolute, period, and cycle-to-cycle jitter — and ends with the bandwidth U-curve that the designer trades against. The recurring punchline: the sub-100 fs budget — 87.6 fs / −51.6 dBc in our model, ≈ slide-42's 87.5 fs / −51.7 dBc slide p42 — is not arbitrary; it is the number that simultaneously satisfies a 1024-QAM transmitter and a far-out reciprocal-mixing mask.

1. Jitter → EVM → QAM SNR

A modulator with random phase wander smears each constellation point. For small angles the smear is just the phase itself, so the RMS error-vector magnitude equals the RMS phase in radians.

Intuition

Picture a 1024-QAM constellation: 1024 dots packed into a square. A symbol at unit radius, rotated by a small random angle $\phi$, lands a distance $\approx \phi$ (rad) away from its ideal spot — that displacement is the error vector. The fraction of symbols that cross into a neighbor's decision region is set by how big $\phi$ is relative to the dot spacing. Denser constellations (more bits/symbol) have tighter spacing, so they demand a smaller $\sigma_\phi$ — equivalently a more negative IPN.

Math

For small phase error, the error vector on a unit-power symbol is $\approx \sin\phi \approx \phi$, so $$\boxed{\;\text{EVM}_\text{rms}\approx \sigma_\phi\ (\text{rad})\;}$$ and the phase-noise-limited SNR is the inverse of that error power: $$\text{SNR}_\text{phase}=-20\log_{10}(\sigma_\phi)=-\text{IPN}_\text{DSB}\quad(\text{dB}).$$ derived/model Mind the convention. $\text{SNR}_\text{phase}=-20\log_{10}\sigma_\phi=-10\log_{10}\sigma_\phi^2$ is minus the total-variance (DSB) IPN — and the slide-3 QAM specs are quoted DSB, so for them SNR $= -\text{IPN}$ directly. But the deck's headline IPN is the SSB form $10\log_{10}(\tfrac12\sigma_\phi^2)$, which is 3 dB lower: so $\text{SNR}_\text{phase}=-\text{IPN}_\text{SSB}-3\,\text{dB}$. For 87.6 fs that is SNR $=48.6$ dB while $\text{IPN}_\text{SSB}=-51.6$ dBc (not $-48.6$). Keep them straight: SNR tracks DSB; the $-51.6$ dBc headline is SSB. slide p42 convention

2. Reciprocal Mixing (the receiver's view)

EVM is the transmitter's complaint. The receiver has a different, often stricter, complaint that lives at far offsets where the EVM integral has long since died: a strong adjacent blocker mixes with the LO's skirt and dumps noise right on top of your wanted signal.

Intuition

The LO is not a clean tone; it has phase-noise skirts. When a big out-of-band blocker hits the mixer, it convolves with that skirt and produces a noise floor inside your channel — as if the blocker had been "painted" with the LO's phase noise and slid down to baseband. This is reciprocal mixing. Crucially it is set by $\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)$ at the blocker offset, which can be tens of MHz away — a region governed by the VCO's far-out (thermal) floor, not by the in-loop jitter that dominates EVM. So this spec is frequently independent of, and orthogonal to, the integrated-jitter EVM spec.

Math

The blocker's contribution to in-channel power is its power, lowered by the LO phase noise at the blocker offset, integrated over the channel bandwidth: $$\boxed{\;\frac{C}{I}=P_\text{wanted}-\big[P_\text{blocker}+\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)+10\log_{10}\!\text{BW}\big]\;}\quad(\text{dB})$$ textbook with $\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)$ in dBc/Hz and BW in Hz. The $10\log_{10}\text{BW}$ converts the per-Hz skirt into the total noise power folded into the channel.

3. Jitter Figure of Merit

How do you compare a 60 fs / 50 mW synthesizer against a 90 fs / 14 mW one? You can't with jitter alone — you need a metric that prices in power. The jitter FoM does exactly that, and it encodes a hard thermal law: jitter² × power is a constant.

Intuition

Every fundamental noise source (kT/C sampling, VCO tank thermal noise) scales as $1/\text{power}$: spend 2× the current and you cut the noise power in half, i.e. jitter by $\sqrt2$. So the product $\sigma_t^2 \cdot P$ is roughly invariant for a given technology and topology — it is the "exchange rate" between jitter and watts. The FoM just takes its log in dB.

Math

$$\boxed{\;\text{FoM}_\text{jitter}=10\log_{10}\!\Big[\big(\sigma_t\big)^2\cdot\frac{P}{1\,\text{mW}}\Big]\;}\quad(\text{dB, more negative = better})$$ textbook (Gao/Staszewski FoM) For this design, $\sigma_t=87.6$ fs and $P=14.2$ mW (VCO 12 + DTC 1.2 + MMD 0.5 + rest 0.5) slide p39: $$\text{FoM}=10\log_{10}\!\big[(87.6\times10^{-15})^2\cdot 14.2\big]=-249.6\ \text{dB}.$$

Sanity check. Every move that follows the thermal law \(\sigma_t^2 P=\text{const}\) leaves the FoM flat (a slide along the iso-FoM diagonal); only breaking that law (same jitter, less power) moves you to a lower diagonal. The −3 dB of the dual core is a noise win, not an efficiency win.

4. Jitter Taxonomy: Absolute vs Period vs Cycle-to-Cycle

"Jitter" is three different numbers depending on what you difference. They all come from the same phase-noise PSD — period and cycle-to-cycle jitter are just high-pass-weighted versions of the absolute spectrum.

Intuition

  • Absolute (long-term) jitter — how far each edge has wandered from an ideal grid. This integrates the full PSD including the low-frequency hump, so it is the largest. This is what EVM and IPN use.
  • Period jitter — the variation of one cycle's length: difference adjacent edges, $\Delta t_n = t_{n+1}-t_n$. Differencing is a $(1-z^{-1})$ operation, which high-pass weights the PSD, killing the slow wander. Smaller number.
  • Cycle-to-cycle jitter — the variation between consecutive periods, a second difference $(1-z^{-1})^2$. Even more aggressively high-pass; matters for things like SerDes timing recovery and digital setup/hold.

Math

Period jitter applies a first-difference weighting to the absolute phase PSD: $$S_\text{period}(f)=\big|1-e^{-j2\pi f T_0}\big|^2\,S_\phi(f),\qquad T_0=1/f_\text{out}.$$ The factor $|1-e^{-j2\pi f T_0}|^2 = 4\sin^2(\pi f T_0)$ rolls off as $f^2$ at low offset — that is the high-pass weighting that suppresses the close-in hump. Cycle-to-cycle squares the weighting again. derived/model textbook (period/c2c jitter)

5. The Design Tradeoff: REF-vs-VCO Bandwidth U-curve

Every PLL designer fights the same battle. Open the loop bandwidth and you suppress more VCO noise but pass more reference/DTC noise; close it and you do the reverse. The total integrated jitter is a U-shaped function of bandwidth, with a single optimum.

Intuition

The loop is a low-pass for everything entering at the reference (REF phase noise, DTC quantization + thermal, the divider/DSM path) and a high-pass for VCO noise (Section 1's NTFs). Widening the bandwidth $f_c$:

  • lets the loop track and suppress more VCO noise (good — VCO is 51% of the budget slide p42),
  • but admits more of the REF + DTC noise into the output band (bad — REF+DTC is ~39% slide p42).

The crossover of these two trends is the U-curve minimum. Slide 42 explicitly notes the bandwidth "is chosen to trade off REF vs VCO — common to all PLLs." slide p42

Putting it together

Sub-100 fs is not a vanity number. It is the intersection of four independent pressures: dense-QAM EVM (Section 1) pulls the in-band IPN down to −44…−47 dBc; reciprocal mixing (Section 2) pulls the far-out VCO floor down toward −154 dBc/Hz at the blocker; the jitter FoM (Section 3) prices every one of those dB in milliwatts, with the dual-core VCO as the one lever that buys jitter without buying efficiency; the taxonomy (Section 4) reminds you which "jitter" the spec actually means; and the U-curve (Section 5) is where the designer finally lands — 1.5 MHz / 87.6 fs, two femtoseconds shy of optimum, by choice. The DTC-assisted architecture exists precisely to make that 87.6 fs reachable at 14.2 mW instead of paying the brute-force thermal tax.
