Interactive Lab — learn by doing

The rest of the site explains the DTC-assisted fractional-N PLL; this page is where you poke it. Below is a guided path of short missions — each poses a question, points you at the right interactive widget, and folds the answer you should observe. Five hero widgets are embedded right here; the rest are one click away on their home pages. Work top-to-bottom the first time, then use the widget index at the bottom as a launchpad.

How to use a mission. Read the Question, form a guess, then do the Try step in the widget. Only then open What you should see and argue with it. Every number quoted is reproduced live by the same models the widgets run model; the deck's own values (87.5 fs / −51.7 dBc, slide 42) are kept distinct from our model's (87.6 fs / −51.6 dBc).

Theme 1 — The architecture & the budget

Start with the punchline: a clean sub-100 fs budget that exists only because the DTC cancels the ΔΣ quantization noise. Toggle that cancellation off and watch the whole thing fall apart.

Mission 1 — Why does this architecture exist at all?

Question: The total is 87.6 fs. How much of that is the ΔΣ quantization noise — and what happens to it without the DTC?

Try: in the pie above, untick DTC cancels DSM-QN.

What you should see
With cancellation on, the ΔΣ slice is a rounding-error 0.26% of the budget. Untick it and the DSM slice explodes and the total leaps to thousands of fs — the loop is unusable. That one toggle is the reason the DTC-assist architecture exists: it turns a fractional-N loop back into an integer-N-clean one. model

Mission 2 — Is the loop bandwidth optimal?

Question: The deck runs at \(f_c=1.5\) MHz. Is that the lowest-jitter choice?

Try: open the budget optimizer → and drag the loop-bandwidth slider to find the U-curve minimum.

What you should see
The minimum is at \(f_c^\star\approx2.1\) MHz giving ≈85.6 fs — the design sits slightly left of optimum (1.5 MHz / 87.6 fs), trading ~2 fs of jitter for extra phase margin and spur suppression. Below the optimum the VCO term dominates; above it the REF+DTC term (×\(N^2\)) takes over. model

Theme 2 — Loop dynamics & lock

How a phase margin you dial becomes an overshoot you feel — and where the continuous-time picture stops being trustworthy.

Mission 3 — Phase margin ↔ overshoot

Question: Set PM to 45° and then 70°. Which is "twitchy" and which is "sluggish"?

Try: drag the phase margin slider in the widget above.

What you should see
PM 45° → ζ≈0.71, gain peaking ~3 dB, ~33% overshoot (lightly damped, rings). PM 70° → ζ≈1.4, ~0.9 dB peaking, ~17% overshoot (well damped, flat). The design's 60° (ζ=0.97, 1.70 dB, ~25%, 1.13 µs settle) is the deliberate middle. model

Mission 4 — Can you just "crank \(f_c\)" to kill VCO noise?

Question: A reviewer says "push \(f_c\) to \(f_\text{ref}/8\approx13\) MHz." What breaks?

Try: open ZOH erosion → and slide \(f_c\) toward 13 MHz.

What you should see
The sampled-loop ZOH lag \(-\pi f/f_\text{ref}\) is a harmless −2.6° at 1.5 MHz but eats −22.5° at \(f_\text{ref}/8\), collapsing PM from 60° toward ~37°. And (Mission 2) you'd be past the jitter optimum anyway. Conclusion: keep \(f_c\) low-single-digit MHz; beat the VCO with cores, not bandwidth. model

Mission 5 — Why two integrators and a zero?

Try: open type-I vs type-II → and compare the closed-loop \(|H_\text{ref}/N|\).

What you should see
Type-II (2 integrators + zero) peaks 1.70 dB — the zero is what stabilizes it but also what makes it peak. Type-I (1 integrator) is flat (no peaking) and has a luxurious ~83° PM, but it can't null a frequency error. Fractional-N needs type-II's zero-error tracking, so you accept the peaking. model

Theme 3 — Calibrations (the heart of the design)

Four background sign-LMS loops keep the DTC cancellation honest over PVT. This is the deepest part of the site; the embedded widget is the keystone coupling.

Mission 6 — Why must the offset cal converge first?

Try: in the widget above, switch between offset cal OFF, concurrent, and offset FIRST.

What you should see
With offset never corrected, the K_DTC gain loop locks to the wrong +22.6% (an 80-LSB offset under ½-range leaks via \(E\{\Phi_e\}=0.25\)). Run them concurrently and the gain is yanked toward that bad value then slowly recovers. Converge offset first → the gain marches straight to target. That's why the offset DC-servo is the keystone. model

Mission 7 — Fast lock AND a quiet floor?

Try: open gear-shift µ →; compare "single µ" vs "gear-shift µ".

What you should see
A single µ sits on the speed-vs-ripple frontier (\(\tau\cdot\mathcal{M}\approx\)const): big µ = fast but noisy, small µ = quiet but slow. Gear-shift (big µ to acquire, drop to small µ to track) jumps the green ★ below the frontier — fast lock and a quiet steady state, because the two specs are needed at different times. model

Mission 8 — What is cal accuracy worth in femtoseconds?

Try: open residual ε → jitter →; drag ε from \(10^{-4}\) up toward the 0.226 offset-bias value.

What you should see
The added jitter is linear in ε: 4.5 fs at \(10^{-4}\), 45 fs at \(10^{-3}\), 449 fs at \(10^{-2}\) — each decade of accuracy buys a decade of floor. Push ε to the uncalibrated-offset value 0.226 and the floor blows ~100× past the 87.6 fs budget. This is why the cals must reach ε≲\(2\times10^{-4}\). model

Mission 9 — Do the four loops fit in 30 µs?

Try: open the 4-loop dashboard →.

What you should see
The time-constant spread tracks how structured each regressor is: the ±1-keyed VCO/CKREF duty loops settle in <1 µs, K_DTC gain in ~4 µs, and the offset DC-servo — whose only signal is the tiny DC mean of a 1-bit stream — is the slow keystone at ~15 µs. All clear the 30 µs budget. model

Mission 10 — Same cubic, which basis converges?

Try: open monomial vs Legendre →.

What you should see
Both bases span the same cubic predistortion, but the monomials \([D,D^2,D^3]\) are correlated (\(E\{D\cdot D^3\}=1/5\)) so their \(R\) is ill-conditioned and the residual plateaus; orthogonal Legendre \(P_1,P_2,P_3\) give a diagonal \(R\) and converge to ~11× lower residual. Same orthogonality lesson as the four-loop argument, now inside one loop. textbook

Theme 4 — Fractional spurs & the ΔΣ residue

Where a fractional channel's periodic residue becomes discrete tones — and the three levers that crush them to the measured −70…−85 dBc.

Mission 11 — Drive the spur into the noise

Try: in the widget above step the range from full → ½ → ⅛, then tick DTC NLC.

What you should see
Worst spur −56 dBc (full) → −68 (½) → −92 (⅛) — ≈12 dB per halving, because the dominant INL term is quadratic (\(40\log_{10}r\)). Add NLC and it drops below the −135 dBc plot floor ("cancelled"). That ladder is exactly how the silicon reaches −70…−85 dBc. model

Mission 12 — When are spurs worst?

Try: open the DSM residue + PSD explorer →; set the MASH order and a near-rational FCW like 0.10.

What you should see
The PSD floor rises toward Nyquist with slope ≈\(20m\) dB/dec for MASH-\(1^m\) (the \((1-z^{-1})^m\) shaping); raising the order drops the in-band floor but doubles the time-domain range. A near-rational FCW (0.10) makes the residue periodic, collapsing the PSD into tall discrete lines — spurs are worst exactly when the residue is most periodic. model

Related: the range-reduction ledger → (one DR lever moves spur −12 dB, QN −6 dB, power ÷4 per halving).

Theme 5 — System impact

What the femtoseconds actually buy at the antenna: constellation EVM, blocker rejection, and the jitter–power figure of merit.

Mission 13 — Does 87.6 fs close a 1024-QAM link?

Try: in the widget above set the jitter to 87.6 fs and read the EVM.

What you should see
87.6 fs at 6.72 GHz → \(\sigma_\phi=3.70\) mrad → 0.37% EVM / 48.6 dB phase-SNR. 1024-QAM needs −44 dBc (44 dB), so you have ~4.6 dB of margin and even clear the 4096-QAM −47 dBc line. model

Mission 14 — Why won't retuning the loop fix reciprocal mixing?

Try: open reciprocal mixing →, put the blocker far out, then drag the loop bandwidth.

What you should see
Reciprocal mixing is set by \(\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)\) at the blocker offset (tens of MHz), where \(H_\text{vco}\to1\) — the far-out VCO thermal floor. Retuning \(f_c\) reshapes the in-band skirt but barely moves \(\mathcal{L}(\Delta f)\), so the C/I hardly changes. It's an orthogonal spec to EVM. model

Mission 15 — Does a dual-core VCO improve the FoM?

Try: open the FoM scatter → and toggle the dual-core VCO.

What you should see
A dual core drops VCO PN 3 dB (≈62 fs) but at ~2× power, so the star slides along the same iso-FoM diagonal — lower absolute jitter, unchanged FoM (−249.6 dB). Real efficiency means moving to a lower diagonal — exactly what the DTC-assist (better jitter at the same power) achieves. model

Mission 16 — A second, independent path to 88 fs

Try: open the time-domain noise lab → and toggle sources on/off.

What you should see
This runs the actual discrete loop sample-by-sample in your browser; with all sources on it lands near 88 fs, independently confirming the frequency-domain budget by a completely different method. Toggle the VCO off and watch the in-band floor drop and the spectrum change shape (the loop high-passes the VCO, low-passes the rest). model
